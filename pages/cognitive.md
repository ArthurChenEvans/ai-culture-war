# The AI Culture War — Cognitive Explanation

**Kos'myna · Dahmani & Bohbot · Fan et al. · Meta-analysis · Cognitive reserve**

## The Cognitive Explanation

An honest look at the "AI makes you dumber" argument — distinguishing use-it-or-lose-it weakening from structural damage, engaging the MIT study cited for cognitive decline and the much stronger meta-analytic evidence pointing the other way, and asking the only question that actually matters: not whether you use the tool, but how.

## 1. The Claim and Why It Needs Scrutiny

A claim has propagated through online discourse with remarkable speed: using AI makes you cognitively worse. It rots your brain. It creates "cognitive debt." It produces a generation of intellectually hollow people who cannot think without a machine. The claim is delivered with the certainty of settled science, and the research most often cited to support it is a study from the MIT Media Lab that found reduced brain activity in participants who used ChatGPT to write essays.

This essay exists because I owe a correction. In the Ethical Explanation, I wrote — correctly in spirit but too loosely in language — that "people who rely on GPS forget how to read maps. People who rely on calculators lose mental arithmetic speed. People who rely on spell-checkers become worse spellers." I used the word "atrophy" to describe what happens. That framing survived a reader challenging it, which forced me to go back and examine whether the evidence actually supports the analogy and what kind of atrophy is being described. It does, and it matters.

What the evidence actually supports is considerably more specific and considerably less dramatic than the popular narrative. The "AI makes you dumber" claim conflates three different things: use-it-or-lose-it weakening of specific skills (real, dose-dependent, reversible), structural brain damage (not supported by any evidence), and cognitive reserve erosion over decades (a legitimate long-term concern that applies to any chronic cognitive disuse, not to AI specifically). Sorting these out changes the argument considerably.

## 2. What "Atrophy" Actually Means

The word atrophy does a lot of work in AI discourse, and it is usually doing the wrong work. In medical usage, atrophy covers a spectrum. At one end is the kind of atrophy that happens when you stop exercising a muscle: the tissue shrinks, neural pathways weaken, performance declines. Start exercising again and it comes back, usually within weeks for short disuse periods. This is use-it-or-lose-it atrophy and it is the normal behaviour of a healthy body adapting to the demands placed on it. At the other end is the kind of atrophy seen in neurodegenerative disease: cells die, tissue is lost, and the damage does not reverse with training. These are fundamentally different phenomena with fundamentally different implications.

When people invoke the GPS-atrophies-your-hippocampus argument against AI, they are usually imagining the second kind — something closer to brain damage than to a gym habit. The evidence supports the first kind. This distinction is not a semantic quibble. It determines what the concern actually licenses. If AI use produces use-it-or-lose-it weakening, the response is "practice the skill periodically, be thoughtful about what you offload, and you will be fine." If AI use produces neurodegeneration, the response is "this is a public health emergency and we need regulation at the level of tobacco control." The popular rhetoric argues for the second response based on evidence that only supports the first.

> A useful way to think about this: your calves do not become permanently damaged if you stop walking for a month. They weaken, and start walking again and they recover. Whether forty years of sedentary living leaves you with less reserve capacity when you hit seventy is a different and more serious question, but it is not the same question as "does skipping the gym today cause irreversible damage tomorrow."

## 3. The GPS Evidence — What It Actually Shows

The GPS parallel is the most frequently invoked analog for AI-induced cognitive decline, and it is worth examining in detail because the underlying research is unusually strong. Dahmani and Bohbot (2020), published in Scientific Reports, combined a cross-sectional study of 50 regular drivers with a longitudinal follow-up of 13 participants tested three years later. The findings were consistent and mechanistically coherent: greater lifetime GPS experience correlated with worse spatial memory during self-guided navigation, poorer cognitive map formation, and encoding of fewer environmental landmarks. More importantly, the longitudinal data showed that greater GPS use between the two testing points predicted a steeper decline in hippocampus-dependent spatial memory — establishing the causal direction as GPS use leading to decline, not pre-existing poor navigators gravitating toward GPS.

The mechanism the authors propose is informative for our purposes. Spatial navigation can be performed by two competing brain systems: a hippocampus-dependent system that builds cognitive maps of landmark relationships, and a caudate-nucleus-dependent stimulus-response system that learns sequences of motor responses (turn left at the next corner). GPS trains the second system at the expense of the first. The spatial memory decline is not "damage" to the hippocampus; it is the hippocampal strategy being progressively out-competed by the shortcut-taking strategy. The dose-dependent pattern — more GPS use, more decline — is the signature of use/disuse weakening, not toxic exposure.

Crucially, the evidence also shows the phenomenon is reversible. Maguire et al.'s landmark 2000 study of London taxi drivers, who spend years memorising the city's street network to pass The Knowledge, found that the posterior hippocampus grew larger with years of experience. Bunarjo et al. (2020) ran a four-week real-world spatial exploration intervention with older adults and found significant improvement on hippocampus-dependent memory tasks compared to controls. A 2024 cohort study of 8.9 million US death records found that taxi and ambulance drivers — occupations requiring real-time spatial navigation — had the lowest Alzheimer's-related mortality of any of 443 occupations analysed; bus drivers and pilots on predictable routes showed no such protection. The hippocampus is responsive to use in both directions. Skip the gym and it weakens. Go back to the gym and it comes back.

> This does not make the concern trivial. Chronic disuse over decades may reduce what neuroscientists call cognitive reserve — the buffer of functional capacity that allows the brain to tolerate age-related changes before deficits become clinically apparent. Someone who spent forty years never navigating without GPS and never doing any other hippocampus-engaging activity may have less reserve when they hit seventy than someone who spent those forty years actively navigating. This is a real, long-term concern. It is not the same concern as "GPS causes brain damage." And it applies equally to any chronic cognitive disuse, from never reading books to never doing puzzles to never having intellectually demanding conversations.

## 4. The MIT ChatGPT Study — What It Found and Why It Does Not Say What Headlines Claim

Nataliya Kos'myna and colleagues at the MIT Media Lab released a preprint in June 2025 titled Your Brain on ChatGPT: Accumulation of Cognitive Debt when Using an AI Assistant for Essay Writing Task (Kos'myna et al., 2025). Fifty-four participants from the Boston area, aged 18 to 39, were assigned to one of three groups: an LLM group using ChatGPT, a Search Engine group using Google, and a Brain-only group using no tools. Each group wrote three SAT-style essays across three sessions while electroencephalography (EEG) recorded brain activity across 32 regions. A fourth session switched the LLM group to brain-only (LLM-to-Brain) and the Brain-only group to LLM (Brain-to-LLM); only 18 of the original 54 completed this session.

The headline findings: LLM users showed the weakest brain connectivity across the three sessions. When switched to brain-only in session four, the LLM-to-Brain group showed reduced alpha and beta connectivity, which the authors interpret as under-engagement. LLM users reported the lowest sense of ownership over their essays and struggled to accurately quote their own work. These findings were reported in mainstream outlets under headlines declaring that ChatGPT "rots your brain" and causes "cognitive decline."

The problem is that the study does not support those headlines, and the principal author has been unusually vocal in saying so. Kos'myna's own project page explicitly asks journalists to avoid the phrases "brain scans," "LLMs make you stop thinking," "brain damage," and "terrifying findings." She has said her urgency to publish the preprint before peer review was motivated by a concern that "in six to eight months, there will be some policymaker who decides, let's do GPT kindergarten" — a concern about rollout speed outpacing research, not a declaration that ChatGPT damages brains. The paper itself is explicit that findings are preliminary and limited by a small participant pool recruited from a narrow geographic area near several large academic institutions, a single task (SAT essays), a single LLM, and a sample of 18 for the critical session-four comparison.

A methodological critique from researchers at the Centre for Change and Complexity in Learning at the University of South Australia (The Conversation, 2025) identifies a more serious problem than sample size. The Brain-only group did the essay task three times and got better at it — a phenomenon known as the familiarisation effect. When the LLM group finally wrote an essay without tools in session four, they were doing the task for the first time. They matched the Brain-only group's first-session performance but not their third-session performance. This is exactly what one would expect from the familiarisation effect alone, independent of any cognitive-debt mechanism. To justify the stronger claim the researchers make, the LLM-to-Brain group would have needed three tool-free sessions, not one. The comparison as designed cannot separate the effects of AI use from the effects of practice.

> None of this means the study is worthless. It is a reasonable preliminary investigation that raises hypotheses worth testing more rigorously. What it does not do is establish that AI use causes measurable cognitive decline. That stronger claim requires peer review, replication with larger samples, controlled comparisons that eliminate the familiarisation confound, and extension beyond a single essay-writing task with a single tool. Treating the Kos'myna preprint as a settled finding is bad epistemics, and the popular framing — which the author herself has explicitly disavowed — is worse.

## 5. The Meta-Analytic Evidence Points the Other Way

Meanwhile, a body of research considerably larger than any single study has been accumulating on the effects of AI tools on learning and cognitive performance. Fan et al. (2025), published in Humanities and Social Sciences Communications, conducted a meta-analysis of 51 research studies published between November 2022 and February 2025. The findings: ChatGPT had a large positive effect on learning performance (g = 0.867), a moderately positive effect on learning perception (g = 0.456), and a moderately positive effect on higher-order thinking (g = 0.457). A Hedges' g of 0.867 is a large effect size in educational research. A separate meta-analysis of 35 experimental studies covering 4,193 participants found a moderately positive effect on student learning outcomes (g = 0.670), significantly enhancing both cognitive and non-cognitive skills.

These findings are not uniformly positive, and the moderating variables matter. Fan et al. found that the effect on learning performance varied by course type, learning model, and duration. Interventions shorter than one week showed weak effects — students lacked the skills to prompt effectively. Interventions longer than eight weeks showed a slight decline in benefit, consistent with a developing over-reliance effect. The sweet spot is roughly two to eight weeks of structured use, at which point students have learned how to use the tool productively but have not yet offloaded so much that their own engagement declines. This is not a pattern of "AI makes you dumber." It is a pattern of "AI produces measurable learning benefits when used as a learning tool rather than a task-completion substitute."

The critical qualifier, acknowledged across this literature, is that AI tools reduce mental effort (Wang et al., 2024). Whether this reduction produces positive or negative outcomes depends entirely on what the user does with the freed-up cognitive bandwidth. Redirecting it to harder problems produces gains. Pocketing it as a pure effort reduction on an unchanged problem set produces losses. This is the same dynamic the University of South Australia critique identified with calculators: calculators did not make students worse at mathematics because curricula raised the bar to include more complex computation that calculators made tractable. If curricula had kept requiring the same long-division by hand after calculators arrived, calculators would have produced exactly the "cognitive debt" the Kos'myna paper reports.

## 6. Mode of Use Is the Variable That Matters

Aggregating across this evidence base — the GPS findings, the Kos'myna preprint, the two meta-analyses covering 86 studies between them, and the broader literature on cognitive offloading — one variable consistently determines outcome: how the tool is used. The same piece of software, used two different ways, produces opposite effects on cognition.

Tool-as-tutor use preserves and often enhances cognitive engagement. The user asks the AI to explain a concept, walks through the explanation with active questioning, identifies gaps in their understanding, and builds a mental model that the AI's output supports but does not replace. The user does the thinking; the AI compresses the time cost of accessing relevant information. This is functionally similar to what happens when a human tutor is effective — the tutor does not do the student's thinking, but provides scaffolding, examples, and correction that accelerate the student's own cognitive work. The meta-analytic positive effects cluster in this mode.

Tool-as-substitute use produces the pattern the Kos'myna paper identifies. The user asks the AI to produce a finished artefact — an essay, a summary, a piece of analysis — and skims, pastes, or lightly edits the output. The user is not engaging the underlying cognitive task; they are engaging a quality-control task of judging whether the output is acceptable. Skip that judgement step, and the user has outsourced the whole thing. The Kos'myna participants who "struggled to accurately quote their own work" were not displaying cognitive decline in the clinical sense. They were displaying the predictable consequence of never having engaged with the text deeply enough to form stable memories of it.

The calculator comparison holds here too, but it cuts the other way from how it is usually invoked. A student who uses a calculator to perform arithmetic they already understand, in service of a more complex problem, retains the underlying arithmetic skill and extends their range of solvable problems. A student who uses a calculator as a black box without ever understanding multiplication loses nothing because they never had the skill, but also fails to develop number sense that would have served them later. Neither student has been cognitively damaged by the calculator; they have been shaped by different uses of the same tool. AI produces the same structure of outcomes on a considerably larger cognitive surface area.

> This is where the earlier felleanimated framing of AI as "teacher or tester" captures the distinction correctly. AI used as a teacher — explaining, scaffolding, providing feedback — tends to strengthen the user's own capability. AI used as a tester of completed work, where the user does the thinking and the AI verifies or critiques, also tends to strengthen capability. AI used as a doer — where the human outsources the cognitive task entirely and merely selects between candidate outputs — tends to weaken the specific skill being outsourced. The tool is the same. The mode of use is the variable.

## 7. The Long-Term Question: Cognitive Reserve

There is one version of the concern that deserves more serious engagement than the popular "AI rots your brain" framing allows, and it is the cognitive-reserve version. Cognitive reserve refers to the brain's capacity to tolerate age-related pathology before functional deficits become clinically apparent. Two people with identical Alzheimer's pathology on post-mortem imaging can have wildly different lived experiences — one may have displayed progressive dementia for years, the other may have functioned normally until the week before death. The difference is reserve, and reserve is substantially built by decades of engaged cognitive activity: education, demanding work, complex social interaction, novel problem-solving, language learning, musical practice, and yes, active spatial navigation.

If AI becomes a chronic substitute for cognitive effort across decades — if the average person in 2060 has spent forty years outsourcing reading, writing, summarisation, analysis, and decision-making to an AI assistant — then the cognitive-reserve question is serious. We would be looking at a population that spent their entire intellectually formative adulthood using a tool that reduces mental effort, with no countervailing demand that they redirect the freed-up bandwidth to harder problems. The question is not "will this generation fail to learn to read," which is silly; the question is "will this generation arrive at seventy-five with the reserve to compensate for normal age-related brain changes." This is an honest concern and the evidence base on it will not be settled for decades because the longitudinal data cannot exist yet.

But the cognitive-reserve concern does not license the popular rhetoric, for two reasons. First, it is not AI-specific. The same concern applies, with the same evidentiary strength, to anyone who spends decades consuming short-form video in preference to reading, to anyone whose work is purely routine, to anyone socially isolated, to anyone avoiding novel experiences. Treating AI as a uniquely threatening technology in this space is not supported; it is one of many modern convenience technologies that reduce cognitive load, and probably not even the most potent. Second, the mode-of-use distinction applies here too. Someone who uses AI to engage with denser material than they could previously handle is building reserve, not eroding it. Someone who uses AI to avoid engaging with anything challenging is eroding reserve whether or not AI exists — the AI is an accelerator of a habit they would have exhibited regardless.

## 8. What the Evidence Actually Licenses

Pulling the threads together produces a picture that supports neither the "AI makes you dumber" rhetoric nor the "AI has no cognitive downsides" rhetoric. What the evidence actually supports is a considerably more mundane set of claims, all of which the sensible reader probably suspected before they read this essay.

Specific skills you stop practicing will weaken. This is trivially true and well-documented across cognitive domains. It applies to arithmetic when calculators appeared, spelling when spell-check appeared, spatial navigation when GPS appeared, and writing when AI appeared. The weakening is use-dependent, dose-dependent, and reversible with practice. It is not brain damage.

The mode of use determines the direction of the effect. Tool-as-tutor use produces learning gains; tool-as-substitute use produces effort reduction without skill development. This is true of AI, calculators, GPS, and essentially every other cognitive tool humanity has invented. The variable is not whether the tool exists but what the user does with it.

The strongest popular claim has the weakest evidence behind it. "MIT found that ChatGPT rots your brain" rests on a preliminary, unpublished, small-sample study with a documented methodological confound whose own author explicitly rejects the reductive framing. Meanwhile, meta-analytic evidence covering tens of studies and thousands of participants shows that structured AI use produces moderate-to-large positive effects on learning outcomes. The popular narrative and the aggregated evidence point in opposite directions.

Cognitive reserve is a legitimate long-term concern, and not an AI-specific one. Decades of chronic cognitive disuse produce measurable impacts on late-life cognition. This is true whether the disuse is caused by AI, short-form video, socially isolated routine work, or any other pattern that reduces engagement with cognitively demanding activity. Singling out AI as the driver is not supported by the evidence, and is frequently used to avoid engaging with the broader and more awkward question of how modern life in general affects the engaged brain.

## 9. The Usual Conclusion, One More Time

The other essays in this series have argued that the responsibility for structural problems — environmental strain, copyright erosion, labour displacement, memory-market cannibalisation — belongs at the institutional level rather than the individual level. This one is different. The cognitive question is the one place where the individual user is actually the appropriate target of attention, because the variable that determines the outcome is how the user chooses to engage with the tool, which is a variable the user and only the user can control.

But this does not produce a case for moralising at other people about their AI use. It produces a case for personal reflection about your own. If you are using AI as a tutor — asking it to explain, to critique, to correct, to extend your range — you are probably fine, and may well be learning faster than you would without it. If you are using AI as a substitute — generating essays, summaries, or analysis you do not read carefully and do not understand — you are building a habit whose long-term consequences are worth thinking about, the same way someone who watches six hours of short-form video a day might reflect on what they are not doing with those hours.

The pro-autonomy thesis of this essay series is that choices about how to live are personal choices, and that moralising at others about theirs is the wrong response to virtually every problem in this space. The cognitive essay is not an exception to that thesis. Your cousin using ChatGPT to write birthday cards is not making your brain worse. Your own habits of engagement with AI may be making your brain better or worse depending on what you do, and that is a question for you to sit with honestly, not a question on which you are obligated to perform publicly for an audience of strangers.

> The popular "AI makes you dumber" argument has the structure of moral panic dressed as neuroscience. It takes a real phenomenon (use-it-or-lose-it weakening of specific skills) and inflates it into brain damage. It takes a preliminary study with known methodological problems and treats it as settled science. It ignores the larger meta-analytic evidence that points the other way. And it uses all of this to justify treating individual AI users as moral failures, when the actual scientific question — does this mode of use, for this user, at this dose, produce gains or losses? — is one that only the individual user can meaningfully engage with.

The pencil did not rot your ancestors' brains. The calculator did not rot your parents' brains. The GPS did not rot your brain, though it did weaken a specific skill that is recoverable with practice. AI, on current evidence, behaves like every other cognitive tool: it amplifies what the user brings to it. A thoughtful user gets a thoughtful outcome. A lazy user gets a lazy outcome. And the variable that separates the two is not whether the user opened ChatGPT, but what they did after they opened it.
