What happens to art when machines can make it? Does creativity require intention? Is prompt writing a skill? What the photography precedent, the chess analogy, the Hollywood strikes, and the anime industry's ambivalence tell us about the future of human creativity. Not anti-art. Not anti-AI. Anti-gatekeeping.

1. What Is Creativity, Anyway?

Before we can ask whether AI is creative, we need to be honest about how poorly defined "creativity" is even as applied to humans. Common definitions cluster around a few ideas: novelty (producing something new or unexpected), intentionality (acting with purpose or vision), emotional resonance (producing work that affects an audience), and authenticity (the work expresses something genuine about the maker's experience or perspective). The problem is that these criteria are in tension with each other and inconsistently applied. A human artist who deliberately copies an existing style is often considered creative (homage, genre work, pastiche). A human who produces novel work driven primarily by market research is often considered less creative than one following pure artistic vision. Children's scribbles are called "creative" by loving parents and dismissed by galleries.

Psychologist Margaret Boden distinguishes three types of creativity: combinatorial (combining existing ideas in new ways), exploratory (exploring the boundaries of an established conceptual space), and transformational (fundamentally changing the rules of a conceptual space). AI currently excels at the first two. The third — genuine paradigm-breaking transformation — remains contested. But the deeper issue is that the culture war rarely engages with these distinctions. "Creativity" is deployed as a tribal marker, not an analytical category. When someone says "AI isn't creative," they typically mean "AI doesn't belong to my tribe" — which is a social claim, not a philosophical one.

2. The Photography Precedent — We Have Been Here Before

When photography was invented in the 1830s, painters predicted the death of art. A machine that could capture reality with perfect fidelity would render the painter obsolete. The argument was identical in structure to the modern anti-AI position: a mechanical process was replacing a human skill, the output was aesthetically comparable, and the practitioners of the older method would lose their livelihoods. The panic was real, and the fears were not entirely unfounded — portrait painters did lose significant market share to photographers.

But painting did not die. Freed from the obligation to produce realistic depictions, painters explored new frontiers of expression — impressionism, expressionism, abstraction, surrealism. Photography's arrival did not destroy art; it transformed it, creating both a new art form (photography itself) and liberating the old one from constraints that had limited its expressive range for centuries. The parallel to AI is direct: if AI can generate competent images from text descriptions, human artists are freed from the obligation to produce commodity illustration, and can focus on the creative decisions, the conceptual vision, and the deeply personal expression that only a human perspective can provide.

The photography precedent also demolishes the "it takes no skill" argument. Early photographers were dismissed as mere button-pushers by painters. Today, nobody seriously claims that Ansel Adams or Annie Leibovitz were unskilled because they used a camera rather than a brush. The tool is different; the skill is real. Mobile photography did not kill professional photography — it created a new mass-market tier while elevating the premium tier. AI art is following the same trajectory.

3. The Miyazaki Line — When Opposition Becomes Iconic

In 2016, Hayao Miyazaki — co-founder of Studio Ghibli, director of Spirited Away, arguably the greatest living animator — was shown an early AI-generated animation system. His response has become perhaps the most quoted anti-AI statement in any creative field: he called it "an insult to life itself." Watching the jerky, uncanny movement produced by the system, Miyazaki — who was at the time managing a progressive illness — said quietly that he could not watch it without feeling this was "a huge disservice to people who are really suffering."

The irony that followed is almost too perfect: by 2025, "Ghibli-style" AI image generation had become one of the most popular aesthetic trends on social media. Millions of people used AI tools to generate images in the distinctive hand-painted style that Miyazaki and his team spent decades perfecting through painstaking manual labour. The viral Ghibli AI filters simultaneously demonstrated two things: the enormous cultural power of the aesthetic Miyazaki created, and the absolute indifference of the broader market to the source artist's explicit wishes. The people generating these images were not, for the most part, malicious. They were fans expressing their love for the aesthetic in the way the technology made available to them. The ethical weight of this — explored in the Ethical Explanation — is real. But so is the cultural reality.

4. The SAG-AFTRA / WGA Strikes — AI as a Labour Issue

The 2023 Hollywood strikes — the Writers Guild of America (WGA) from May to September, and SAG-AFTRA from July to November — were the first major industrial actions in which AI was a central and explicit negotiating point. Both unions won significant protections: the WGA secured language preventing studios from using AI to write or rewrite scripts (though writers can use AI tools themselves if they choose), and SAG-AFTRA negotiated restrictions on the digital replication of actors' likenesses and voices without consent and compensation.

The strikes established a template that other creative industries are now watching: AI in creative work is not primarily a philosophical question about creativity, but a labour question about who gets paid, who gets credit, and who controls the means of production. A screenwriter replaced by an AI tool loses income whether or not the AI's output is "truly creative." This framing cuts through the metaphysical fog: whatever you believe about machine consciousness or artistic authenticity, the economic displacement is real, measurable, and addressable through negotiation and regulation. The solution is not to ban AI, but to ensure that the humans whose work trained the models and whose jobs are affected by them are treated justly in the transition.

5. The Anime Industry — A Case Study in Ambivalence

Nowhere has the AI art debate been more intense, nuanced, and globally watched than in Japan's anime and manga industries. A 2026 survey found that 89% of professional Japanese artists viewed generative AI as a "serious threat" to their livelihoods. The "No AI Learning" movement — artists marking their work to protest training data harvesting — originated in Japan after the launch of the Mimic AI tool in 2022, which could replicate individual artists' styles from just 10 sample images. The backlash forced Mimic to shut down within 24 hours.

Yet the same industry has also seen internal debates about pragmatic adoption. Some manga artists have acknowledged using AI for background generation, time-consuming grunt work, or initial composition sketches. The distinction between "AI as a tool used by an artist" and "AI replacing an artist" is contested and often blurry. Japan's government copyright guidance — which is notably more permissive of AI training than EU or US frameworks — has complicated the picture further. The anime industry embodies the central tension of the creative debate: genuine fear and genuine utility coexisting in the same profession, often in the same individual.

6. Prompt Writing as Descriptive Writing — The Skill Nobody Wants to Acknowledge

The Ethical Explanation covers the prompt-as-skill argument in detail, but its creative implications deserve separate treatment here. Prompt writing is not a new skill — it is a specific application of one of the oldest skills in human culture: descriptive writing. When you write a novel, you cannot use visual imagery directly. You must use vocabulary that is specific, imaginative, and precise enough to cause the reader to visualise the scene you intend. You must learn how to weave words together coherently, to deploy metaphors and analogies that ground abstract scenes to something comprehensible. This skill takes years to develop. An expert writer consistently conjures similar mental imagery across multiple readers. A novice produces vague, inconsistent impressions.

Prompt writing is precisely this skill, aimed at a different audience. Descriptive writing in storytelling targets the human imagination. Prompt writing in AI image generation targets a machine's generative process. The underlying competency — precision, vocabulary, coherence, clarity — is identical. A less obvious but equally relevant analogy is flashcard creation in the Anki community: learning to craft flashcards that produce high retrieval success rates is a skill requiring knowledge of what to write, how much to write, and how to target the answer without overloading information. It takes months of practice to do well. The audience is different (your future self, rather than a reader or a machine), but the skill of writing precisely to achieve a specific cognitive outcome is the same.

To deny that prompt writing is a skill is, by consistent logic, to deny that book writing, teaching, speechwriting, marketing, and every other form of descriptive communication is a skill. It is to claim that the ability to articulate a vision clearly and precisely — the foundation of all creative direction — takes no effort. This is self-evidently absurd, and the people making the claim are usually those who have never watched an expert prompt artist at work.

7. The Chess Analogy — Why the Best Humans Will Become More Valuable

Chess has never been more computationally solved than it is today. A chess engine running on a mobile phone can defeat Magnus Carlsen. We no longer need IBM's Deep Blue or a warehouse-sized supercomputer to outplay any human who has ever lived. Chess is, for all practical purposes, a solved game. And yet chess has never been more popular. Viewership is at all-time highs. Prize pools are growing. Young players are entering the game in record numbers. The existence of superhuman engines has not killed human chess — it has made it more interesting, because what audiences want to see is not optimal play (they can get that from an engine), but human ingenuity, improvisation, and the drama of a mind at work under pressure.

The prediction for creative professions follows exactly this pattern. The average photographer may be pushed out of business by AI-generated imagery — the same way the average portrait painter was pushed out by the camera. But the truly exceptional photographers — those with a genuine eye for capturing a moment, a distinctive perspective, a personal vision — will become more valuable, not less. Audiences saturated with AI-generated slop will hunger for authenticity. They will be hit in the face by something genuinely human and go: that person was awesome. They will desire human ingenuity over the statistical median. The premium tier does not shrink when the commodity tier is automated. It grows.

This is cold comfort for the median practitioner who is being displaced right now. And the transition period — the years during which the market sorts itself out — will be painful for many people. The Ethical Explanation addresses the labour and transition questions. But the long-term trajectory, based on every historical precedent we have, points toward a world where human creativity is more valued, more visible, and more distinct from machine output than it has ever been. The "death of art" is not coming. The transformation of art — as with every previous tool revolution — has already begun.

8. The Authenticity Question — Does Process Matter?

Perhaps the deepest objection to AI-generated art is not about quality, or originality, or economics — it is about authenticity. Art, on many accounts, derives its meaning not just from what it depicts or how it sounds, but from the fact that a human being made it: that a human consciousness engaged with the world, had an experience, struggled to express something, and produced a work as the trace of that struggle. If this account is correct, then AI-generated output is not art in any meaningful sense — it is a sophisticated simulacrum, aesthetically indistinguishable from the real thing but ontologically hollow.

The counter-view holds that meaning is constructed by audiences, not transmitted by artists. A work means what audiences find in it, regardless of how it was made. On this view, if AI output moves, challenges, or delights an audience, it has succeeded as art — full stop. The audience does not need to know the provenance of the work to be affected by it, and the effect is not diminished by the knowledge that a machine produced it (or, at least, it should not be, any more than learning that a beloved painting was created by a forger diminishes its aesthetic qualities, even if it changes its market value).

The honest answer is that both views have merit, and neither can be definitively proved. What we can say is that the authenticity argument, taken to its logical conclusion, proves too much: it would deny artistic status to any work produced without conscious suffering, including commissioned commercial illustration, corporate logo design, and pattern-book architecture — all of which are produced "mechanically" by human practitioners following well-established formulas for market purposes. If authenticity requires struggle and personal expression, most human-made commercial art fails the test too. The line between "authentic human art" and "mechanical human production" is far blurrier than the anti-AI movement acknowledges.

The question is not whether AI can produce beautiful things. It clearly can. The question is whether beautiful things produced without experience, intention, or suffering mean the same thing as beautiful things produced with all of those — and whether that distinction matters more than the aesthetic impact on the audience. The Philosophical Explanation explores this through the lens of qualia and consciousness. Here, in the creative domain, the practical answer is: it depends on who is looking, and what they are looking for. Both answers are valid. Neither is the whole truth.

9. The Verdict: Transformation, Not Extinction

The anti-AI position in the creative sphere wants to frame the debate as an extinction event: AI will kill art, kill artists, and produce a world of soulless machine-generated slop. The pro-AI position wants to frame it as pure liberation: AI will democratise creativity, free artists from drudgery, and usher in a golden age of human expression. Both framings are wrong because both are absolute.

The historical pattern — from the printing press to the camera to the synthesiser to desktop publishing — tells a consistent story: transformative tools displace median practitioners, create painful transition periods, generate new forms of creative expression, and ultimately elevate the premium tier of human craft by making authenticity and human vision more distinctive and more valued. AI is following this pattern with unusual speed, which makes the transition more painful and the fear more acute. But the trajectory is the same.

Three realities must be held simultaneously. Creatively, AI produces outputs that are aesthetically competent, sometimes striking, and occasionally indistinguishable from human work — but it does not (yet) produce paradigm-breaking transformational creativity, and it is fundamentally limited by its nature as a statistical pattern engine operating on learned distributions. Economically, the displacement is real and the affected people deserve support, negotiation, and just transition — not dismissal by either tech optimists or anti-AI absolutists. Culturally, the most skilled human artists will thrive in an AI-saturated world for the same reason that chess masters thrive in a world of superhuman engines: because audiences want to see human minds at work, and the contrast with machine output makes the human element more visible, not less.
