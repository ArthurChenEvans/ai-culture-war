The Environmental
Explanation

A comprehensive, data-driven analysis of AI's true environmental impact — synthesising the global water crisis data, national grid strain evidence, the generative AI greenwashing debate, and the computing efficiency argument. Not anti-AI. Not pro-AI. Anti-waste.

1. The Global Water Crisis Is Real — But AI Did Not Cause It

In January 2026, the United Nations University Institute for Water, Environment and Health (UNU-INWEH) published a landmark report declaring that the world had entered an era of "global water bankruptcy" (UNU-INWEH, 2026). This report was immediately seized upon by anti-AI commentators as evidence that artificial intelligence — and the data centres that power it — were driving the planet toward ecological collapse. The claim was dramatic: AI is draining our water supply, and we have roughly 13 years before catastrophe.

The problem is that the UNU-INWEH report says nothing of the sort. Nowhere in the document does it blame AI or data centres as a primary cause, or even as a major driver of the crisis. Instead, it explicitly points to decades-long human factors that predate the AI boom by half a century or more: chronic groundwater over-extraction, agricultural overuse accounting for roughly 70% of global freshwater withdrawals, deforestation, pollution, soil degradation, urbanisation, and climate change compounding it all (UNU-INWEH, 2026). Many of the world's major aquifers and river basins have been overdrawn for more than 50 years. This trajectory was locked in long before anyone had heard of ChatGPT.

The UNESCO World Water Development Report (2024) confirms this picture with striking clarity. Agriculture accounts for roughly 70% of fresh water usage globally, followed by industry at just under 20%, and domestic or municipal use at approximately 12% (UNESCO, 2024). The Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations corroborates these figures, stating verbatim that "agriculture remains the largest water-using sector globally, accounting for 72 percent of withdrawals in many regions" (FAO, 2024). Notably, the FAO report goes on to identify the primary drivers of increasing water stress as "rapid demographic growth and agricultural demand," particularly in Western Asia and the Middle East — not data centres, not AI, not computing of any kind (FAO, 2024).

So where does AI actually sit in this picture? According to the Pattern Energy Group and Worldometer data, global AI water consumption as of 2025 stands at approximately 764.6 billion litres, or 0.76 trillion litres (Pattern Energy, 2025). Total global human water usage in 2025 was approximately 616 trillion litres (Worldometers, 2025). That places AI's share at 0.12% of global water usage. To claim that 0.12% is the reason the drinking water supply will collapse in 13 years would be, to borrow an analogy, like claiming that the entire population of Greece (roughly 9.8 million people, or 0.12% of the global population) had suddenly become the sole cause of a worldwide catastrophe. The numbers simply do not support it.

It is worth noting that the UNU-INWEH report itself actually recommends using AI as part of the solution. One of its proposed global agendas is to "embed water-bankruptcy monitoring in global frameworks using Earth observation, AI, and integrated modelling" (UNU-INWEH, 2026). If AI were truly the environmental destroyer that viral social media claims suggest, it is difficult to imagine the United Nations endorsing it as a tool for solving the very crisis it is supposedly causing.

2. The Agricultural Industry Is Not as "Essential" as You Think

A common rebuttal to the data above runs as follows: "Yes, agriculture uses more water, but agriculture sustains billions of people. AI is optional. They are not equivalent categories." This argument sounds intuitive, but it collapses under scrutiny when you examine what the agricultural industry actually does with the water it consumes.

According to the World Wildlife Fund, approximately 40% of all food produced globally — nearly half — is wasted along the supply chain (WWF, 2021). That represents 2.5 billion tonnes of food, or over 5.8 trillion meals. Distributed across the global population of 8 billion, that is nearly one thousand meals per person per year going to waste. The Eco Experts calculate that this wasted food alone could feed over 5 billion people — more than 66% of the world's population — three meals per day for an entire year (The Eco Experts, 2023). Meanwhile, 828 million people (10% of the global population) go hungry every single day. Energy, water, and greenhouse gas emissions are pouring into trillions of meals that reach no one's stomach.

The waste extends beyond food that rots on shelves. According to the University of Leiden, some 18 billion animals die each year but never end up being eaten (Universiteit Leiden, 2023). Furthermore, the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change reports that globally, 40% of crop calories are used as animal feed rather than for direct human consumption (IPCC, 2019). If those crop calories were redirected to cereal production, they could feed an additional 3.5 billion people per year.

The food waste problem has direct water implications. If agriculture accounts for 70% of global freshwater withdrawals, and 40% of all food produced is wasted, then roughly 28% of all agricultural water — approximately 728 trillion litres — is being spent on food that nobody eats. That wasted water alone is 958 times greater than the entire global water footprint of AI. The methane released by this decomposing food waste has a global warming potential 28–36 times greater than CO₂ over a century (NASA, 2024). The IEA projects that existing AI applications could help reduce CO₂ emissions by 1.4 gigatonnes by 2035 (IEA, 2025a) — AI would directly help fight against this waste.

So when someone argues that AI is an "optional luxury" while agriculture is "essential," the honest response is: a substantial portion of the agricultural industry is not sustaining anyone. It is generating waste on a scale that dwarfs AI's resource consumption by several orders of magnitude.

3. The Luxury Water Test: Coffee, Chocolate, and the 76-Cent Mortgage

If the argument is that AI is "optional" while agriculture is "necessary," then we need to be honest about what we are actually growing. Is chocolate a necessity? Is coffee? No one needs these to survive; they are pure luxuries. Yet their water footprint dwarfs AI by staggering margins.

According to the Swiss Platform for Sustainable Cocoa, approximately 4.4 billion kilograms of cocoa were produced in the 2023/2024 season (Kakaoplattform, 2024). According to the Hong Kong Water Supplies Department's virtual water data, chocolate requires 24,000 litres per kilogram to produce (Water Conservation, n.d.). Global coffee production for 2024/25 is forecast at approximately 174.4 million 60-kilogram bags, or roughly 10.8 billion kilograms, at 18,900 litres per kilogram. The resulting figures are staggering: chocolate production uses 105.6 trillion litres annually (139 times more than global AI), and coffee uses 204.1 trillion litres (268 times more). Combined, these two luxury industries consume over 300 trillion litres of water — roughly 400 times the entire water footprint of global AI.

You can argue that beef is survival food. You cannot argue that about a mocha bar. If AI is "optional slop" that nobody needs, then mathematically, you should be 400 times more concerned about your morning latte than about ChatGPT.

The infrastructure argument yields similar results. According to the IEA and the UNU-INWEH report, aging infrastructure is one of the biggest "bankrupting" forces. Globally, 25–30% of all treated water is lost to leaks in aging pipes before it ever reaches a consumer (IEA, 2025a; UNU-INWEH, 2026). The United States power sector alone withdraws 180 trillion litres of water (APPA, n.d.). If just 10% of the infrastructure supporting that sector is leaky or inefficient, that represents 18 trillion litres of water wasted on absolutely nothing — 23 times larger than the total water footprint of the global AI industry.

To put this in financial perspective: if our total water debt were a one-million-dollar mortgage, agricultural waste from food rot would be $280,000; infrastructure waste from leaky pipes would be $180,000; luxury spending on coffee and chocolate combined would be $77,000. The global AI usage is exactly 76 cents. We are throwing $460,000 into the bin every month through leaks and food rot, spending $77,000 on candy and caffeine, and claiming we are bankrupt because of a 76-cent tool that the United Nations itself recommends using to fix the pipes.

4. Computing Is Efficient — The Masley Argument

Andy Masley, a physicist and former physics teacher, has produced what is perhaps the most thorough data-driven analysis of AI's environmental footprint to date (Masley, 2025a, 2025b, 2026). His central argument is deceptively simple: computing is efficient. The average American spends roughly 7.5 hours per day online — about 50% of their waking hours (Exploding Topics, 2025). Yet data centres, which host and process the entire internet, account for just 4.4% of US electricity consumption (MIT Technology Review, 2025). When you factor in that 66% of America's total energy budget is non-electric (vehicles, heating, industrial processes), the full internet — data centres, transmission infrastructure, and display devices combined — represents roughly 4% of total American energy use (Masley, 2025b). We spend half our lives on something that uses 4% of our energy. That is not a crisis; it is a miracle of engineering.

At the individual prompt level, the numbers are even more striking. The best current estimates put a single ChatGPT prompt at approximately 0.3 watt-hours of energy (Ritchie, 2025). That is enough energy to run a microwave for one second, leave a single LED bulb on for 18 seconds, or play a gaming console for six seconds (Masley, 2025a). The full carbon cost of a prompt — including training amortisation, chip manufacturing, cooling, and the fact that data centres draw from grids that are 48% more carbon-intensive than average — comes to approximately 0.28 grams of CO₂: about one 150,000th of the average American's daily carbon emissions (Masley, 2025a).

Water tells the same story. According to Google's own data, each prompt uses approximately 2 millilitres of water when you include both onsite cooling and the offsite water used to generate the electricity (Masley, 2026). The average American's daily water footprint is 1,600 litres, or 422 gallons (Mekonnen & Hoekstra, 2018). That means every single day, the average American uses enough water for 800,000 chatbot prompts. Manufacturing a single pair of jeans uses enough water for 5.4 million prompts; a smartphone, 6.4 million (Water Calculator, n.d.). If you want to offset the water of a lifetime's worth of AI usage, Masley notes, "just don't buy a single additional pair of jeans" (Masley, 2025a).

All US data centres combined — the vast majority of which support the general internet, not AI specifically — consumed approximately 200–250 million gallons of freshwater daily in 2023, or roughly 0.2% of America's daily freshwater consumption (Potter, 2025). AI's share of that is approximately 20%, putting AI at 0.04% of US freshwater. By 2030, even under the most aggressive growth forecasts, all data centres combined are projected to consume 0.12% of US freshwater — equivalent to 8% of the water consumed by the American golf industry (USGA, n.d.; Masley, 2026).

5. The SpookyFM Correction: Additive, Not Substitutive

SpookyFM, a researcher of 15 years, offers a critical methodological correction to the Masley framework (SpookyFM, 2026). While SpookyFM does not dispute the underlying mathematics, the critique targets the framing. The most important point is the distinction between additive and substitutive usage.

Masley argues that a deep research prompt uses roughly the same energy as microwaving a bowl of popcorn — therefore it is negligible. SpookyFM's response is devastating in its simplicity: "But then we haven't microwaved the popcorn." The point is that for most people, AI usage does not replace a higher-energy activity. It is additional. We are still driving, still eating beef, still wearing sneakers, still microwaving popcorn — and now we are also running AI on top of all of that. In a state of global water bankruptcy, adding a "small" new debt when you are already insolvent is still bad resource management.

SpookyFM also exposes what might be called the "Kate, Bob, and Freddy" fallacy in Masley's work. Masley credits 500 people with "saving a nuclear plant" to make individual lifestyle changes look small by comparison. SpookyFM points out that those 500 people have zero actual control over the nuclear plant; they only have control over their diet and their clicks. The framing uses scale to make individuals feel powerless — a tactic that, whether intentional or not, aligns with the interests of those expanding AI infrastructure.

Finally, SpookyFM challenges Masley's claim that his analysis does not require peer review because he is "just summarising existing numbers." A literature review — which is what Masley's analysis effectively is — is actually harder to do correctly than a primary study, because it requires accounting for biases in the sources being cited, such as the IEA's "tactical vagueness" in failing to distinguish between traditional and generative AI (SpookyFM, 2026).

None of this demolishes the global mathematics. AI is still 0.12% of global water. But SpookyFM's contribution is essential because it prevents the mathematics from becoming a shield: a way for the industry to say "it's just 0.3 Wh" and silence everyone whose national grid is at breaking point.

6. The National Disaster: Where the Numbers Stop Being Abstract

While the global picture makes AI's resource footprint look manageable, zooming in to the national level reveals genuine damage. At this scale, the data centre expansion driven by generative AI is no longer a rounding error — it is a systemic threat to national infrastructure, clean energy transitions, and legally binding carbon budgets.

In Ireland, data centres consumed 22% of the country's total national electricity as of 2024, with projections showing this hitting 32% by 2026 (EirGrid, 2024). Between 2017 and 2023, 100% of all additional wind energy generated in Ireland was absorbed by new data centres. AI is not "helping" Ireland's energy transition; it is eating the progress that homes and transport are trying to make.

In the United Kingdom, the energy regulator Ofgem reported in February 2026 that grid connection enquiries from 140 new data centres total 50 gigawatts — 5GW more than the entire peak demand of Great Britain (Ofgem, 2026). The national grid physically cannot support this growth without abandoning its 2030 clean-power targets.

In the United States, data centre energy consumption jumped to 4.4% of national electricity in 2024 and is projected to reach 12% by 2030 (MIT Technology Review, 2025). In Virginia — "Data Center Alley" — data centres already account for 24% of Dominion Energy's electricity demand (Virginia Mercury, 2024). More than half of all fossil fuel plants in the PJM interconnection market have deferred or cancelled retirement plans specifically because of surging data centre demand (WRI, 2026). Goldman Sachs estimates that of the 82GW of incremental capacity additions to serve data centres through 2030, 51% will come from natural gas peaking plants and only 27% from solar (Goldman Sachs, 2025).

The conclusion is inescapable: stopping generative AI does not "save the planet" globally — the global water level would barely move. But it un-jams the transition nationally. In Ireland, 22% of electricity would suddenly be available for homes and EVs. In Virginia, the coal plants could actually shut down. The weight on the national grid would vanish.

7. The Thermal Wall: Why Water at the Chip Is Now Physics, Not PR

A development that has reshaped the water narrative deserves direct engagement, because it is frequently missed in discussions framed around 2022-era data centre design. Modern AI accelerators have crossed a thermal threshold where air cooling is no longer physically viable. NVIDIA's Blackwell B200 GPU draws approximately 1,000 watts per chip, and the GB200 superchip pairing two B200s with a Grace CPU can reach 2,700 watts. A single Blackwell Ultra rack consumes up to 140 kilowatts — roughly ten times the density that conventional air-cooled halls were designed for (Tom's Hardware, 2025). At these densities, the heat generated per square metre exceeds what moving air can remove, regardless of how aggressively the air is chilled.

This has forced an industry-wide transition to direct-to-chip liquid cooling. The cold-plate approach — a copper micro-channel block mounted directly on the GPU or CPU, with coolant flowing through sealed channels — is now the dominant architecture, holding approximately 65% of the liquid cooling market. NVIDIA has made liquid cooling a standard configuration on the GB200 platform and is jointly developing cold-plate interfaces with major OEMs. As of 2026, approximately 22% of newly built data centre facilities use liquid cooling, and the figure is rising rapidly as Blackwell and Rubin-class platforms ship (KAD, 2026; DatacenterDynamics, 2025).

The critical implication for the water debate: the coolant in these systems runs in closed loops. Water is not consumed in the way evaporative cooling towers consumed it in 2022-era data centres. The coolant circulates through the cold plates, dumps its heat at a heat exchanger, and returns to the chips. Makeup water for losses is marginal compared to the traditional once-through or evaporative approaches that drove the 2022 water-crisis headlines. This is not a PR move by the industry; it is an engineering necessity. Mineral scaling from open-loop cooling would destroy multi-million-dollar GPU packages in weeks. Protecting the hardware required closing the loop, and closing the loop dramatically reduced per-chip water consumption.

This does not make AI water-neutral. Data centres still consume water for humidity control, for chilling plant makeup, and in regions that rely on evaporative secondary cooling. The 0.12% global figure already reflects this. What the thermal transition does is rule out the "AI is going to drain the aquifers" framing that assumed 2022-style cooling infrastructure scaling linearly with compute. The architecture has changed. Discussions still citing the older figures as if they represent current or future designs are out of date.

The local-disaster framing from Section 6 survives this update. Ireland's 22% electricity share, the UK's 50GW grid enquiries, Virginia's data centre load — none of these are about water; they are about electricity, grid capacity, and the opportunity cost of renewables. The thermal transition addresses water specifically, not the national-scale grid stress. The two concerns are separate and the distinction matters for where pressure should be applied.

8. The Nuclear Pivot: The Durable Externality of the AI Buildout

One of the more counterintuitive consequences of the AI infrastructure boom is what it has done to clean baseload energy financing. LLM training and inference clusters require 99.9%-class uptime — intermittent solar and wind, on their own, cannot meet that requirement without either storage at scales that do not yet exist at price, or fossil-fuelled backup that defeats the decarbonisation purpose. This has forced hyperscalers into a position that environmental advocacy has been trying to achieve for decades: becoming the primary financiers of next-generation nuclear power.

The deals are substantial and well-documented. Amazon led a $500 million financing round for X-energy, a gas-cooled small modular reactor (SMR) developer, and has partnered with X-energy and Dominion Energy on deployments targeting more than 5 gigawatts by 2039 (IEEE Spectrum, 2024; WWT, 2025). Google has partnered with Kairos Power targeting a first SMR online by 2030. Meta is funding two TerraPower units (up to 690 MW) and an Oklo campus (1.2 GW). Microsoft restarted Three Mile Island Unit 1. NextEra and TerraPower announced in 2026 a landmark SMR partnership to fuel Google and Microsoft AI data centres, with initial sites in Iowa and the south-eastern United States (Financial Content, 2026).

The realistic timelines are long. Most SMRs will not produce commercial power until 2028–2030 at the earliest, and regulatory and construction risk remain real (WWT, 2025). But the direction of investment is unambiguous. Hyperscaler balance sheets are now providing the offtake certainty and upfront capital that first-of-a-kind nuclear projects have historically struggled to secure. The UK has committed £280 million to Rolls-Royce's 470 MW SMR with private matching, Canada's Darlington BWRX-300 reached construction approval in 2025, and China's Linglong One became the world's first operational commercial land-based SMR in 2023 (Introl, 2025).

This produces an uncomfortable observation for both sides of the debate. For AI critics: the infrastructure buildout that you correctly identify as a short-term driver of fossil-fuel retention in the PJM market and of Ireland's net-zero setback is also, simultaneously, the most substantial private financing source for the carbon-free baseload that environmental movements have wanted for decades. For AI enthusiasts: the nuclear deals do not retroactively clean up the interim period during which data centres extended the lives of coal and gas plants, and the long commissioning timelines mean the near-term grid impact is what it is.

The honest framing: if AI infrastructure investment continues through the late 2020s as planned, it will leave behind a substantially expanded nuclear and SMR fleet regardless of what happens to AI revenue. This is the "durable externality" of the buildout — the part that survives a valuation correction. Fibre-optic cables laid during the dot-com bubble made YouTube possible in 2005. The 2024-2026 SMR financing commitments will, if the projects complete, make firm carbon-free baseload power available to the entire grid by the early 2030s. The AI industry did not set out to decarbonise the grid as a side effect of training large language models, but that is functionally what is happening.

This does not vindicate AI environmentally. The short-term grid damage, the Ireland setback, the Virginia fossil-plant life extensions, the local water stress in drought-affected regions — all remain real and warrant the regulatory and corporate pressure this essay has advocated throughout. But the environmental accounting has to include the whole ledger. The same buildout causing the short-term damage is also financing the medium-term solution. Honest analysis names both.

9. The Bait-and-Switch: Traditional AI vs. Generative AI

The February 2026 report The AI Climate Hoax: Behind the Curtain of How Big Tech Greenwashes Impacts (Beyond Fossil Fuels, 2026) provides what may be the most important distinction in this entire debate: the separation between traditional (narrow/specialised) AI and consumer generative AI.

The report's strongest finding is that 97% of verified climate benefit claims — the irrigation optimisation, the steel manufacturing efficiency, the materials discovery — relate to traditional AI: predictive models and narrow machine learning systems with low energy footprints and high climate utility. Meanwhile, the massive data centre expansion is being driven almost entirely by consumer generative AI: large language models, image generators, and video synthesis tools with massive energy footprints and zero proved climate benefit (Beyond Fossil Fuels, 2026).

Schneider Electric data cited in the report projects that by 2030, energy consumption for generative AI (inference and training combined) will be 13 times higher than for traditional AI (Beyond Fossil Fuels, 2026, Figure 5). The report found zero examples where consumer generative systems (LLMs) led to a "verifiable and substantial level of emissions reductions" (Beyond Fossil Fuels, 2026, p. 15).

This creates the bait-and-switch. Tech companies cite the proven success stories of traditional AI — Google DeepMind's GNoME discovering 2.2 million new crystals (Merchant et al., 2023), Microsoft and PNNL screening 32 million materials down to 18 candidates in a week (BBC, 2024), AI irrigation systems saving 19 billion gallons for South American farmers (The Guardian, 2024) — to justify the expansion of infrastructure that primarily serves consumer generative AI. The report calls this "tactical vagueness": using the 0.12% "wrench" success stories to justify a 13× increase in energy for "generative AI slop" (Beyond Fossil Fuels, 2026).

It is worth noting that the IEA's own April 2025 report, which projects that AI could reduce global emissions by 1,400 megatonnes of CO₂ by 2035 (IEA, 2025b), is guilty of this same vagueness. The IEA groups grid optimisation (narrow AI), generative scientific discovery (narrow generative AI), and consumer LLM usage under a single umbrella. By failing to distinguish between the bicycle and the rocket, the IEA inadvertently gave Big Tech a "blank cheque to pollute" (Beyond Fossil Fuels, 2026).

This does not change the global mathematics. AI is still a tiny fraction of the problem. But it changes the policy argument entirely. We can have the climate-saving traditional AI without the environmentally destructive consumer generative AI. They are separate things, and the industry's conflation of them is, as the report argues, a hoax.

10. The Verdict: Holding Both Truths

The influencers want to say: "AI is the only reason the world is ending." They are lying about the forest. The tech optimists want to say: "AI is a tiny 1.5% slice so it doesn't matter." They are lying about the trees.

By holding both truths — that AI is a global fraction but a national disaster — we arrive at the only honest position in the room. Three concurrent realities must be acknowledged:

Globally, AI is a tiny 0.12% fraction of the water crisis. Agriculture (70%), aging infrastructure (25–30% leak rates), food waste (40% of all production), and decades of groundwater overextraction are the actual causes. The "13-year doomsday clock" was ticking long before ChatGPT existed. Banning AI globally would not move the needle. Even at worst-case 2030 projections, AI would account for approximately 1.5% of global electricity — a slice of a slice.

Nationally, generative AI is a resource parasite. It monopolises renewables (Ireland), rescues coal plants (Virginia), overwhelms grid connection capacity (UK), and threatens legally binding carbon budgets. Stopping consumer generative AI does not save the planet, but it un-jams the national transition — it gives countries the breathing room to actually meet their 2030 targets.

The fix requires surgical precision, not a blanket ban. Support the specialised AI "wrench" that finds pipe leaks, discovers battery materials, optimises irrigation, and could save 517 trillion litres of water per year if deployed globally. Regulate the generative AI "rocket" that consumes 13× more energy for zero proved climate benefit. Stop letting Big Tech use the success of one to justify the expansion of the other. And stop using the "0.3 Wh" mathematics to silence people whose national grids are at breaking point. And recognise that the same buildout causing the short-term grid damage is also financing the medium-term nuclear solution — honest environmental accounting requires naming the whole ledger, not just the half that confirms the prior.

This is the "anti-waste" position. Not anti-AI. Not pro-AI. Anti-waste. We are looking at the million-dollar global debt and the twelve-hundred-dollar national luxury and saying: we need to fix the debt and stop the luxury spending, because both are leading us toward a post-crisis era of bankruptcy.
