The Ethical
Explanation

A systematic stress-test of the ethical arguments deployed in the AI culture war — from both sides. Every major claim is steelmanned, scrutinised, and held to the same evidentiary standard. Not anti-artist. Not pro-corporation. Anti-bollocks.

1. Ignorance Is Not a Moral Failing

The most common ethical claim levelled against users of AI art tools is that using them constitutes a moral failing — that the act of typing a prompt into Midjourney or DALL-E makes you complicit in the exploitation of artists. This framing treats every casual user as if they have read every discourse thread, studied every copyright case, and chosen to proceed out of malice. The reality is far simpler: most people using AI image generators are hobbyists, not professionals. They are tired parents, elderly grandparents, disabled individuals, casual gamers wanting a server banner, and countless other people who simply do not spend their lives immersed in the online AI ethics debate. They are drawn to the convenience and novelty of the tool without realising that a culture war is raging around it.

Surveys from 2023–2025 consistently show that the vast majority of AI-generated art is never published — it is personal tinkering, experimentation, and play. The average AI art user is not a professional encroaching on commercial markets; they are someone who wanted a fun cat picture and discovered, often only after posting it innocently online, that they had apparently committed a grave ethical sin. Calling this a deep character flaw — a "strike one" against their moral standing — is a profound overreaction. It is a mistake, not a manifesto.

The strongest counterargument is the fast-fashion analogy: even if a consumer does not know about sweatshops, their purchase still fuels the system. This is a fair point in principle, but it collapses under the weight of its own logic. If ignorance of supply chains makes you morally culpable, then virtually every person alive is morally culpable for virtually every purchase they make — from the cobalt in their phone to the water in their almonds. The standard, applied consistently, condemns everyone and therefore condemns no one in particular. The more productive response is education, not condemnation: help people understand the landscape, and most will make more informed choices. Shaming them into silence achieves nothing except reinforcing the perception that the anti-AI movement is more interested in moral policing than in actual solutions.

The broader pattern of technology adoption supports this. People adopted photography, digital art tools, and word processors without initial awareness of the job displacements they caused in traditional fields. Society adapted without labelling early adopters as morally bankrupt. AI tools democratise expression for people who lack the means, time, or physical ability for traditional methods. Treating their use as a moral emergency is historically illiterate.

2. The Artisan Hypocrisy — Where Is Your Outrage for the Tailors?

If the core anti-AI argument is that human creativity deserves special protection because it contains "soul" — an irreducible quality of human expression that machines cannot replicate — then a simple consistency test reveals an enormous blind spot. Where is the equivalent outrage for the tailors displaced by sewing machines in the 1800s? For the shoemakers replaced by factory production? For the carpenters whose hand-carved furniture now competes with IKEA? For the chefs whose artisanal cooking is undercut by industrial food processing?

These crafts are not less "soulful" than visual art. A master carpenter who spends weeks hand-joining a table is expressing as much human creativity and care as a painter spending weeks on a canvas. A chef who develops a recipe through years of experimentation is engaged in a creative process indistinguishable in structure from any other artistic endeavour. Yet nobody organises online campaigns against power tools, factory looms, or food processors. Nobody calls a person who buys factory-made shoes "morally evil." The global handicrafts market grew from $740 billion in 2024 to a projected $1 trillion by 2030, proving that "human soul" niches do not merely survive automation — they thrive alongside it.

The counterargument is that art occupies a unique cultural role — it shapes identity and society in ways that furniture does not. But this is arbitrary elitism. All crafts shape culture: tailored clothes signal status, handmade furniture evokes heritage, culinary traditions define nations. Singling out visual art for special protection while ignoring identical dynamics in every other automated craft is not principled ethics — it is the special pleading of a profession that happens to be very good at generating online sympathy.

3. The Pricing Delusion — When £45 Chibis Meet Market Reality

This point requires candour, and candour is warranted when the people demanding moral purity from others are simultaneously pricing average work at luxury-goods levels. The digital commission market, particularly in anime-adjacent spaces, developed a pricing structure over the past decade that bore increasingly little relationship to quality, time invested, or market reality. Simple chibi bust-up portraits — stylised cartoon headshots that take a competent artist one to three hours — routinely commanded £30–50 in online commission spaces. The market sustained these prices through a combination of artificially low supply, a community culture that discouraged price competition, and a customer base of young commissioners who wanted to feel like patrons of the arts.

AI did not "destroy" this market. It popped a pricing bubble that was never sustainable to begin with. Etsy listings confirm that comparable bust-up commissions sell for $9–30 when competition is healthy. A willing buyer might pay £10–20 out of genuine respect for the artist as a human being who deserves compensation for their labour — but £30–50 for average-quality work was always a market distortion, not a fair wage. When the same artists who charged these premiums then turn around and call anyone who uses an AI tool "morally evil," the cognitive dissonance is difficult to ignore.

The strongest counter is that artists face precarious gig economies and that high prices cover irregular income, tools, and the years of skill development that underpin even a "simple" chibi. This is true, and it applies equally to every other precarious profession — freelance developers, independent musicians, self-employed carpenters — none of whom get a moral pass on overpricing simply because their income is irregular. Business sense means setting competitive rates that reflect real market conditions, not demanding that consumers subsidise a pricing fantasy.

4. "Pick Up a Pencil" — The Cruelty of Gatekeeping

When non-artists are told to "just learn to draw" instead of using AI, the advice sounds reasonable in the abstract and cruel in the specific. Consider the 27-year-old who works a full-time job, cooks for himself, exercises daily, maintains personal projects, and sometimes just wants to relax. He thought a particular image would look cool as a server banner. Should he spend 30 minutes to an hour a day — if he can even sustain that on top of his existing workload — for years, just to produce a single one-off image he had in mind? The maths do not add up for a casual, non-career use case.

Consider the 80-year-old grandmother who wanted a cute cartoon of her cat. She may struggle with motor skills. She has no interest in becoming an artist — she likes to garden and care for her kittens. Is she morally failing by using AI for a one-off image? The common rebuttal is to cite inspiring disabled artists who draw with their mouths or toes. These individuals are genuinely impressive, but they chose art as their primary passion. Their extraordinary commitment makes sense for them. It does not follow that every grandmother, parent, or office worker on the planet must demonstrate the same level of dedication for a casual creative whim.

"But PewDiePie learned so much in just a couple of months!" He is retired and has millions. He has the time and money for the best courses and resources. Holding him up as proof that anyone can "just learn" is like pointing to a lottery winner as proof that anyone can get rich. The advice "pick up a pencil" is the artistic equivalent of telling someone who wants a nice dinner that they are morally obligated to become a Michelin-star chef or starve. It is gatekeeping dressed as encouragement.

5. AI Is Not "Killing" Artists — Adaptation Is the Pattern of History

The claim that AI is "killing art" or "killing artists" treats a historical pattern as if it were unprecedented. Every major tool transition in creative history has provoked identical fears: the camera would kill painting, digital tools would kill traditional illustration, synthesisers would kill musicianship, desktop publishing would kill graphic design. In every case, the craft survived and evolved. Carpenters still exist. Tailors still exist. Shoemakers still exist. They command premium prices specifically because their work is handmade in an era of mass production. The global handicrafts market's growth to $740 billion and beyond is empirical proof that automation does not eliminate human craft — it creates a premium tier.

The chess analogy, discussed in the Mechanical Explanation, is directly applicable here. Chess has never been more computationally solved — a phone app can beat Magnus Carlsen — yet chess has never been more popular. The median player is irrelevant against an engine, but the best human players are more celebrated, more watched, and more valued than ever. The prediction for creative professions follows the same pattern: the average photographer may be pushed out by AI-generated imagery, but the truly exceptional photographers — those with a genuine eye for capturing a moment — will become more valuable as audiences saturated with AI slop hunger for human ingenuity and authentic human vision.

The uncomfortable truth that the anti-AI movement refuses to acknowledge is this: if you are genuinely passionate about art, and you place it on the pedestal that the movement demands, then you should be willing to invest the time to become skilled enough that your work commands a premium over AI output. The people who cannot or will not do this — the casual users, the grandmothers, the office workers — are exactly the people the anti-AI movement most enjoys bullying. And the corporations that use AI to cut employment? That is a business decision driven by the same profit motive that has driven every automation decision in history. The moral failing lies with the corporation's labour practices, not with the tool or its users.

6. Pro Artists Already Use AI — As a Tool

The anti-AI narrative paints a picture of AI on one side and artists on the other, but this binary is false. Many of the most accomplished, skilled, and respected professional artists in the world are already using AI as part of their creative workflow. A 2024 survey found that 45.6% of artists believe text-to-image AI will have a dramatic positive influence on creative practices, with 92% reporting they still exert significant control over their outputs. By 2025, 78% of digital artists reported active AI use in some capacity.

Refik Anadol's immersive AI installations have been exhibited at MoMA and Art Basel. Sougwen Chung's robotic collaborators learn her style via neural networks, blending human gesture with machine precision. Jake Elwes explores AI in queer drag portraits exhibited at the V&A. Adobe's Generative Fill is a staple in professional pipelines. Coca-Cola's 2024 AI campaign generated custom artwork while preserving brand identity. These are not amateurs clicking "generate" — they are skilled professionals integrating a powerful new tool into workflows that still require taste, vision, curation, and expertise.

The pattern is identical to photography's history. When the camera was invented, painters predicted the death of art. Instead, photography birthed impressionism — artists, freed from the obligation to produce realistic depictions, explored new frontiers of expression. AI is doing the same thing: freeing professionals from drudgery (backgrounds, initial compositions, rapid ideation) and allowing them to focus on the creative decisions that only a human vision can make.

7. The Developer Parallel — Where Is the Outrage for Code?

If there is one observation that exposes the ethical argument's foundational inconsistency, it is this: AI is displacing developers far faster and more measurably than it is displacing artists — and the developer community is barely complaining. In 2024, the technology sector saw over 150,000 layoffs, up 36% year-on-year. Junior developer roles dropped 20–25% by mid-2025. IT unemployment spiked to 5.7% against a 4% national average. GitHub Copilot and similar tools automate routine coding tasks that previously required human labour. Yet the programming community's response has overwhelmingly been adaptation: 76.5% of developers expect AI's role to grow, 90% actively use AI coding tools, and new roles in MLOps, AI integration, and prompt engineering are emerging to replace displaced ones.

The anti-AI movement's common dismissal of this parallel — "but programming isn't art, it isn't soulful" — is breathtakingly ignorant of what software development actually involves. Tell a researcher who spent months understanding user needs, conducting studies, staying up until 3am fighting a bug, and iterating through dozens of architectural approaches before producing something that genuinely helps people that their work lacks "soul." Tell an app architect who agonised over accessibility, performance, and user experience that their creative process is categorically different from a painter's. The assertion does not survive contact with reality.

And here is the checkmate: the moment you try to defend this distinction, you are forced into a debate about what is and is not "art" and what is and is not "soulful." These are inherently subjective questions. There is no objective definition of art. There is no measurable quantity of soul. The entire ethical argument against AI, insofar as it rests on the unique sanctity of artistic creation, stands on a subjective pillar — and all the objective pillars (pricing, market data, historical precedent, adaptation patterns) collapse when scrutinised. What this means is that the anti-AI ethical position is projecting a subjective view as moral superiority over another subjective view, without objective evidence to support the hierarchy. That is not ethics. That is ego.

It is worth noting the cultural dichotomy here, explored at length in the Mechanical Explanation. The art community is furious; the programming community has largely embraced AI. The same technology, the same mechanism, the same training-data questions — but diametrically opposite cultural responses. If programmers have more to lose economically and yet are less upset, the anger in the art community cannot be straightforwardly about economics. It is about identity, about the perceived sanctity of a particular kind of labour, and about a community that has not yet confronted the historical pattern that every other automated craft has already lived through.

8. Prompt Writing Is a Skill — And Denying It Denies All Descriptive Writing

The dismissal "it takes no effort, you just write a prompt and get an image" is one of the most persistent and laziest tropes in the entire debate. It collapses the moment you watch someone who knows what they are doing. Professional-grade AI art routinely requires 20–200 iterations, reference image uploads, careful negative prompts, region-specific inpainting, layer masking, post-processing in Photoshop, and sometimes custom LoRAs or ControlNet configurations. A 2024 case study of a single high-end AI piece used for a Magic: The Gathering card required approximately 150 generations, manual layering, and traditional overpainting — totalling 18 hours of work.

The underlying skill — prompt writing — is measurable, repeatable, and economically valued. Competitions like the Midjourney Discord "Top 100" show the same names dominating month after month, which is evidence of consistent skill, not luck. Academic papers on prompt programming treat it as a formal discipline: vocabulary size, syntactic structure, weighting syntax, and semantic chaining all produce statistically significant differences in output quality. Companies hire prompt engineers at $200,000–$375,000 salaries, which is market proof that the skill has real economic value.

More fundamentally, prompt writing is a specific application of descriptive writing — the same skill that underpins storytelling, teaching, marketing, and speechwriting. When you write a novel, you cannot use imagery directly; you must use vocabulary that is specific and imaginative enough to cause the reader to visualise the scene you intend. You must learn to weave words together coherently, to use metaphors and analogies that ground abstract scenes to something the reader can comprehend. An expert writer consistently produces similar mental imagery across multiple readers through precision and craft. An amateur produces vague, inconsistent impressions. This skill takes years to develop.

Prompt writing is the same skill with a different audience. Descriptive writing in storytelling aims to get a human to imagine a scene. Prompt writing in AI image generation aims to get a machine to imagine the scene. The underlying competency — precision, clarity, vocabulary, coherence — is identical. To deny that prompt writing is a skill is, by consistent logic, to deny that book writing, teaching, marketing, and every other form of descriptive communication is a skill. Dismissing prompt craft because "you're just typing" is like saying a film director does nothing because they are "just telling people what to do."

The "but it's just RNG" objection held some weight in 2022. By 2025 it is largely solved: fixed seeds, high step counts, identical models, ControlNet, and reference images give skilled practitioners the same control over randomness that a painter has over pigment consistency. And the "90% of AI art is bad" objection actually strengthens the case: 90% of all art, by any method, is mediocre. Sturgeon's Law applies universally. The fact that the top tier of AI art now routinely competes with mid-tier commercial illustration is what frightens people — they are no longer competing against button-pushers, but against a new generation of highly skilled prompt artists working in hybrid workflows.

9. The "Stealing" Myth — How Diffusion Models Actually Work

The claim that "AI steals art" rests on a mental model of how generative AI works that is factually incorrect. The loudest critics imagine that AI image models literally copy and paste original images, store them intact, and spit out mash-ups of existing works. This is not how modern text-to-image diffusion models function. The Mechanical Explanation covers the mathematics of LLMs; here we address diffusion models specifically, since they are the focus of the art-world debate.

During training, a diffusion model takes real images and gradually adds random noise over hundreds of steps until the image becomes indistinguishable from pure static. A neural network learns to predict and remove that noise at each step — essentially mastering abstract statistical patterns like "how edges form," "common colour distributions in skies," or "textures of fur." The model encodes billions of abstract correlations in its weights, not pixel-for-pixel copies of any original image. During generation, the model starts from pure random noise — no original images are involved — and iteratively removes noise, guided by the prompt and the learned patterns. The output is synthesised from scratch based on probabilities. It is probabilistic reconstruction, not retrieval or collage.

The final model — a 2–4 GB file for Stable Diffusion — does not contain any intact images. Researchers have shown that while overfitting can rarely cause near-duplicates (memorisation in large datasets), safeguards like deduplication and proper training mitigate this. The process is analogous to how a human artist studies millions of references over a lifetime and paints something new without tracing any single source. The mechanism is learning distributions of visual patterns, not copying files.

This does not mean there are no legitimate concerns. The Legal Explanation covers the unsettled court cases in detail. But understanding how the technology actually works is essential for an honest ethical debate — and the "it's just a collage of stolen images" framing is as technically illiterate as claiming that a human artist who has ever looked at a reference photograph is "stealing" from the photographer.

10. The Real Villain — Corporate Data Practices, Not the Tool

After systematically examining the ethical arguments, one criticism stands out as genuinely strong — and it targets the corporations, not the technology or its users. The images used to train commercial AI models were, in many cases, scraped from the public internet without the explicit consent of the creators. Publicly accessible does not mean public domain. A photograph posted on DeviantArt without a paywall is still copyrighted. A painting shared on Twitter is still the intellectual property of the painter. The corporations that built the training datasets knew this, or should have known this, and they scraped anyway because the legal landscape was (and remains) unsettled enough to provide plausible deniability.

This is the genuinely valid ethical critique: not that AI as a technology is immoral, not that users who type prompts are complicit in theft, but that the corporations behind the major models built their products on a foundation of unconsented data acquisition, and they should be held accountable for that. The analogy is precise: it is like someone photographing every page of your art book without permission and showing the copies to a student learning to draw. The student learns legitimate patterns and creates new work — the student is not at fault. The fault lies with the person who stole the book.

This framing is the only one that can actually move the discourse forward. It separates the ethical mess of data acquisition from the act of learning and generation. It protects hobbyists and end users — the grandmother prompting a cat portrait is not complicit in corporate scraping decisions. It correctly identifies the power imbalance: Big Tech versus creators, not "AI bros" versus artists. And it points toward actionable solutions: mandatory opt-out mechanisms, transparent training data documentation, licensing agreements, and regulatory frameworks — many of which are already being implemented under pressure from the EU AI Act and ongoing litigation.

History is full of tools built on shaky early data practices that society later formalised. Early search engines indexed the entire web without asking permission. Google Books scanned millions of copyrighted works without consent — and courts ultimately ruled it fair use. The solution was not to ban search engines or book scanning; it was to develop norms, regulations, and licensing frameworks. The same trajectory is the most plausible outcome for AI training data — and the corporations that resist this transition deserve every ounce of criticism they receive.

11. The "AI Makes You Dumb" Study — And Why It Does Not

A frequently cited piece of "research" claims that generative AI causes cognitive atrophy — that using ChatGPT makes you objectively less intelligent. This study, commonly attributed to "MIT," deserves scrutiny before it is used to dehumanise anyone.

The study tested only 54 participants in total, with the critical final session — where participants switched between AI-assisted and unassisted conditions — containing just 18 people. This sample size is nowhere near sufficient to generalise findings to the broader population with any statistical confidence. The methodology was not a long-term longitudinal study but a single-session experiment using EEG to measure brain activity during a standardised test. Participants either took the test without help, with Google, or with ChatGPT. The finding — that unassisted test-takers used more brain activity and remembered more of their answers — is approximately as surprising as discovering that closed-book exam takers use more mental effort than open-book exam takers. Of course they do. That is what "open-book" means.

Furthermore, the study was a preprint — not a peer-reviewed publication. Citing a preprint as established science is roughly equivalent to citing a Wikipedia edit that has not yet been verified. And the institution was not MIT proper, but MIT Media Lab, which is a for-profit organisation funded by corporate subscriptions — not the same as the government-grant-funded MIT. The previous director of MIT Media Lab resigned due to close ties with Jeffrey Epstein, who was a major funder. This does not invalidate the research, but it should temper the confidence with which it is cited as gospel.

The actual phenomenon being described — reduced cognitive effort when external tools are available — is well-documented and applies to every tool humanity has ever invented. People who rely on GPS forget how to read maps. People who rely on calculators lose mental arithmetic speed. People who rely on spell-checkers become worse spellers. In every case, the solution is periodic review or relearning of the underlying skill — not a permanent reduction in intelligence. Using LLMs may atrophy certain cognitive habits in the same way that using any labour-saving tool atrophies the manual skill it replaces. It does not make you "dumber." It shifts where you allocate cognitive effort.

12. The Existential Risk — Fear, Proportion, and What the Experts Actually Say

Beyond the art and labour debate, there is a deeper, quieter fear: that AI represents an existential risk to humanity. That sufficiently advanced AI could become uncontrollable, deceptive, or hostile — and that the consequences could be catastrophic. This fear is not irrational. It is shared by serious researchers, including Turing Award winners. But the discourse around it has become dangerously detached from what the evidence actually shows.

Yoshua Bengio, a Turing Award winner and one of the "godfathers" of deep learning, cites a survey of AI researchers suggesting that the median expert prediction for an extinction-level risk from AI is approximately 5% — not the 20–25% figure that circulates in anxious online communities (Bengio, 2024, citing Thousands of AI Authors on the Future of AI, 2023). Five percent is not zero. It is a serious probability that warrants serious action. But it is also not a coin flip, and treating it as an imminent certainty is not supported by the expert consensus.

The work being done to address this risk is substantial and often underreported. Bengio chaired an International Scientific Report on the Safety of Advanced AI involving 30 countries, the UN, and the EU, which categorises risks such as loss of control and bio-weapons to enable government regulation (Bengio, 2024). He also launched a non-profit called LawZero, which funds research into moving away from agentic AI (AI with autonomous decision-making capacity) toward what Bengio calls "Scientist AI" — systems that can understand and explain the world without having the agency to act on it or deceive us. LawZero is working on mathematical proofs to ensure that certain AI systems literally cannot take harmful actions (Bengio, 2025).

The 2026 edition of this international report, involving even more experts and countries, found that while AI is improving rapidly at narrow tasks like mathematics and coding, current systems are "insufficiently reliable" to pose a loss-of-control threat. They struggle with long-term planning and are derailed by trivial obstacles like website pop-up advertisements. The rogue AI scenario — which requires sustained, multi-step, real-world planning and execution — is beyond current capabilities. Additionally, 12 major AI companies (including OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google) and 17 Chinese companies have signed safety pledges with publicly committed "tripwires": if a model reaches a certain danger threshold, they have promised to halt deployment. Governments in the EU and elsewhere are working on converting these voluntary promises into binding law.

The honest position on existential risk is neither dismissal nor panic. A 5% median probability of catastrophic outcomes is high enough to justify significant investment in safety research, international coordination, and regulatory frameworks. It is not high enough to justify paralysing fear or the abandonment of a technology that the same experts believe could help solve climate change, disease, and material scarcity. The fear is valid. The proportion matters.

13. The Verdict: Subjective Pillars and Objective Rubble

The anti-AI ethical position, as it is most commonly articulated online, rests on a series of claims that sound powerful in isolation but collapse under systematic scrutiny. Users are not morally evil for typing prompts — they are mostly ignorant hobbyists. The "human soul" argument is applied selectively to art and ignored for every other automated craft. Commission pricing was inflated long before AI arrived. "Pick up a pencil" is gatekeeping that ignores the lived reality of most people. AI is not "killing" artists any more than cameras killed painters. Pro artists are already using AI. Developers are being displaced faster with less complaint. Prompt writing is a measurable, economically valued skill. Diffusion models learn patterns, not copy files. And the one genuinely strong ethical critique — corporate data scraping without consent — targets the companies, not the technology or its users.

The entire ethical argument, as deployed in the culture war, stands on a subjective pillar: the belief that visual art occupies a uniquely sacred position in human culture, and that its creation process is categorically different from all other forms of creative labour. Every objective pillar — pricing, market data, historical precedent, adaptation patterns, technical mechanism — falls when examined. This does not mean there are no legitimate ethical concerns about AI. There are: corporate accountability, consent, labour transition support, and existential risk management are all real and urgent. But the version of the ethical argument that dominates social media — the one that calls grandmothers morally evil for generating cat pictures and strips anyone who disagrees of their intelligence and humanity — is not ethics. It is projection of subjective values as moral superiority, without objective evidence to support the hierarchy.

Both sides hold valid arguments. AI art involves genuine consent issues rooted in corporate data practices. AI is also genuinely capable of creating novel outputs it has never seen before — it does not do it in the same way as humans, but it generates real patterns and concepts from learned distributions. Both ideas can be true at once: it is novel creation, but it learned how to create from data that was acquired without proper consent. The mature position acknowledges both, directs accountability where it belongs (at the corporations), and stops dehumanising the millions of ordinary people who just wanted a picture of a cat in a birthday hat.
