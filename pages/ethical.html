Autonomy · Default · Harm · Scope · Effort · Complicity

The Ethical
Explanation

The default for any technology-adoption choice is moral neutrality. Shifting that default requires demonstrated, proportionate harm — and the burden of proof sits with whoever wants to load the choice morally. This essay examines the serious harm arguments against AI use and against AI refusal, engages each on its strongest form, and asks whether they succeed in moving the default. Not anti-artist. Not pro-tech. Pro-autonomy.

1. The Default and Who Has to Move It

Most technology-adoption choices sit in a default zone of moral neutrality. Using or not using a smartphone, a dishwasher, a car, a credit card, a search engine, a streaming service — none of these is treated as morally loaded absent specific aggravating factors. The default is not "all technology is good" or "all technology is harmless." The default is that the individual choice to adopt or refuse is not itself a moral question, even though the technology may have ethical dimensions that are addressable through other means (regulation, corporate accountability, collective bargaining, personal boycotts where warranted). Loading every consumer choice morally would make ordinary life impossible.

Generative AI has, in certain quarters, been aggressively moved out of that default zone — in both directions. Using it is said to make you complicit in theft, labour displacement, environmental damage, corporate exploitation, or the degradation of human creative practice. Refusing it is said to make you a Luddite, a virtue-signaller, someone missing the future. Both framings try to attach heavy moral weight to a choice most people treat roughly the way they treat whether to use a word processor versus handwriting. Neither framing is obviously right. Both are making substantive ethical claims that require defending against the default of neutrality.

This essay takes the burden of proof seriously. It does not assume neutrality as a conclusion, and it does not dismiss the harm arguments as bad faith. It asks, for each of the serious arguments: does this argument, in its strongest form, succeed in moving the default? The answer reached after examining them is that most do not, and the ones that come closest apply only to narrow edge cases. For the vast majority of actual users and non-users, neutrality holds. But the argumentative work is what gets you there — not the conclusion presented as a premise.

Scope: this essay's central case is non-commercial personal use — hobby projects, casual experimentation, personal productivity, writing assistance, image generation for fun or learning. The neutrality claim is strongest here. It weakens as the use case approaches commercial competition with identifiable human creators, and weakens sharply in cases of targeted style imitation of living artists. Sections 7 and 8 handle those edge cases honestly. Sections 2 through 6 handle the arguments that try to load the casual use case itself.

2. Who Is Actually Using These Tools?

The empirical baseline matters because discourse about "AI users" often constructs an imaginary target that bears little resemblance to the actual user base. The St. Louis Fed's Real-Time Population Survey found that 54.6% of US adults aged 18–64 had used generative AI by August 2025, with personal (non-work) adoption reaching 48.7% — higher than work adoption at 37.4%. Eurostat's 2025 figures show 32.7% of EU people aged 16–74 used generative AI tools, with 25.1% for personal purposes, 15.1% for work, and 9.4% for education. These categories overlap because the same person can use AI across multiple contexts.

Ofcom reports that among UK generative AI users, 48% said they use it "for fun" and 23% for learning or information-seeking. Salesforce's research finds 38% of users use it for fun or experimentation and 34% for learning. Menlo Ventures' 2025 survey of 5,000 US adults found 45% of Baby Boomers aged 61–79 had used AI in the past six months, with 11% using it daily. Globally, DataReportal's Digital 2026 report estimates more than one billion people now use AI tools regularly.

The picture is not a tech-bro profiting by displacing artists. It is a population spanning every age group, using these tools heavily in personal contexts, with casual entertainment and learning dominating the stated motivations. This does not by itself establish neutrality — popularity is not moral correctness. But it means the harm arguments must work against the actual user base, not a caricature. A moral case calibrated to "someone commercially replacing named living artists" cannot simply be redirected at a grandmother making a birthday card and retain its force. The shift in target changes the causal chain, the foreseeability, the alternatives, and the proportionality.

3. The Complicity-in-Corporate-Wrongdoing Argument

The strongest argument that AI use is not neutral runs roughly: the major AI models were trained on datasets scraped from the web without the explicit consent of creators; using these models creates consumer demand that sustains the companies that built them; therefore, using the models makes the user complicit in uncompensated extraction from artists. This is worth taking seriously, because the underlying corporate practice is a genuine ethical problem (addressed in depth in the Legal Explanation).

The argument's weakness is not in the harm claim — the consent issue with training data is real — but in the attribution of that harm to individual users. In a peer-reviewed analysis of consumer complicity, philosopher Michael Deem examines cases exactly like this: a consumer buying from a company engaged in wrongdoing, where the individual purchase makes a vanishingly small causal contribution to the wrongdoing. His conclusion is careful: the individual purchase may make one "a little bit complicit" in a technical sense, but that degree of complicity does not license a large moral sanction. The magnitude of the personal moral charge has to track the magnitude of the individual causal contribution, not the magnitude of the system one has marginally participated in.

This is empirically how we already handle every other consumer-complicity case. Fair-trade certification, dolphin-safe tuna labelling, the Kimberley Process for diamonds, REACH chemical regulation, fashion transparency indices — every one of these mechanisms exists because consumers cannot reasonably police global supply chains individually, and because individual-scale moral responses are the wrong tool for corporate-scale harms. We do not treat driving a petrol car as a moral failure despite the driver's marginal contribution to catastrophic climate change. We do not treat buying from Amazon as a moral failure despite its warehouse labour record. The response to diffuse complicity has always been structural: regulation, disclosure, collective action, and targeted corporate boycotts — not individual moral loading.

The complicity argument, properly examined, does not succeed in moving the default for individual AI use. It succeeds at directing moral weight elsewhere: at the corporations that built the training pipelines, the regulators who failed to establish rules, and the platforms that operated without consent mechanisms. That is where the ethical weight actually belongs, and that is where constructive pressure can actually move outcomes. The Legal Explanation tracks the litigation and regulation that is already doing this work.

4. The Labour-Displacement Argument

A distinct harm argument: using AI contributes to the displacement of human creative and knowledge workers, and therefore users are participating in a collective action that damages livelihoods. This is a serious claim and worth engaging directly rather than dismissing.

The argument has two failures. First, the individual causal contribution of casual users to labour displacement is essentially zero. The economic shift is driven by enterprise adoption, platform-level integration, and corporate workflow decisions — not by hobbyists generating images for fun. A grandmother making a birthday card does not displace a commercial illustrator; an enterprise procurement decision does. The argument, when correctly scoped, targets corporate adopters and platform operators, not casual users. This is the same pattern as the complicity argument: a real harm, but one whose causation runs through institutional actors, not individual consumers.

Second, the argument, if accepted at the individual level, proves too much. By the same logic, buying mass-produced furniture rather than commissioning hand-carved pieces makes you complicit in the displacement of carpenters; buying ready-made clothes rather than hiring a tailor makes you complicit in tailor displacement; using any machine-made good rather than its artisanal equivalent makes you complicit in the displacement of artisans. We do not apply this standard consistently because it would be unlivable. The response to technology-driven labour displacement throughout modern history has been collective bargaining, industry-level protections, just-transition policy, retraining, and social insurance — never individual consumer moral accountability. The 2023 Hollywood strikes are the template: unions secured enforceable AI protections through negotiation with studios, not by shaming viewers who use AI-enhanced products.

The labour argument is legitimate. The labour harm is real. But individual-user moral loading is not the appropriate response to it, and does not actually address it. The response that does address it is institutional.

5. The Human-Flourishing / Effort-Substitution Argument

This is the hardest argument against AI use and the one that deserves the most careful engagement. It does not depend on training-data consent or labour economics. It runs like this: creative effort has intrinsic value for the person performing it, not merely instrumental value for producing output. When AI substitutes for that effort, something is lost even if the output is equivalent — a habit of sustained attention, a relationship with a craft, a mode of engagement with the world. Widespread AI adoption may, over time, be corrosive to human practice in the way GPS navigation has measurably atrophied human spatial cognition. This is a virtue-ethics argument about human flourishing, and it does not dissolve under proportionality analysis the way the complicity and labour arguments do.

Two things are worth saying about this argument before engaging it. First, it is right about something. There is good empirical evidence that externalising cognitive work atrophies the underlying capacity — this has been documented for GPS and spatial reasoning, for calculators and arithmetic fluency, for spell-checkers and orthographic memory. There is no reason to expect AI to be uniquely exempt from this pattern, and some reason to expect the effect to be larger given the broader range of cognitive work AI can absorb. Second, the effect is a public-health-type phenomenon, not a personal moral failing. Finding that GPS use is associated with atrophied spatial cognition does not make any individual GPS user morally wrong; it makes the aggregate cultural shift something worth monitoring and possibly counter-balancing.

The argument, taken seriously, establishes a reason for individuals to think carefully about their own AI use — whether they are substituting for activities they value in themselves, whether they are maintaining the skills they want to keep, whether they are using AI as a tool that augments their work or as a replacement that displaces it. This is a personal-discernment question, not a moral-judgement question. It is the kind of reflection a thoughtful person brings to any labour-saving technology. It does not tell any other individual what to do, and it does not license treating AI users as morally deficient. The analog is the person who chooses to walk rather than drive for fitness reasons: the reasoning is sound, the personal choice is good, but extending that reasoning into moral judgement of drivers would be a category error.

Where the flourishing argument does have moral teeth is at the level of cultural design — how schools integrate AI, how workplaces structure tasks, how parents guide children's use, how individuals structure their own practice. These are serious questions that deserve serious attention and are not resolved by either "AI is neutral" or "AI is evil." The essay's claim is narrower: the flourishing argument does not move the default for the individual-use question. It gives users reasons to reflect on their own patterns. It does not give others grounds to judge them.

This section is where the "pro-autonomy" framing does the most work. The flourishing argument, if turned into a moral rule imposed on others, collapses into paternalism — the view that people must be protected from technology choices that might, statistically, diminish their cognitive flourishing. Autonomy, properly understood, includes the right to make choices that affect one's own cognitive and creative development, including choices others might consider suboptimal. The same autonomy that protects the AI user from being judged for using it also protects the non-user from being judged for refusing it.

6. The Attacks on Non-Users

It is worth being direct that this essay defends non-users too — and also honest that the attacks on non-users are structurally weaker than the attacks on users, and so the defence of non-users takes less work. The common charges against non-users include: Luddism, virtue-signalling, hypocrisy (using some technology but refusing this one), missing out on productivity, and being "left behind." None of these rises to the level of a harm argument the way the complicity or labour arguments do. They are mostly claims about the non-user's own self-interest or about their signalling behaviour, not about third-party damage.

Personal autonomy in technology adoption is a well-established principle. The person who quietly declines to use a new tool because they prefer the old one, because they distrust the companies making it, because they find it aesthetically unappealing, because they worry about their own cognitive patterns, or for no stated reason at all — this person is exercising the same autonomy that protects any other consumer choice. The decision to refuse AI is as neutral as the decision to adopt it, and for the same underlying reason: neither choice imposes meaningful harm on identifiable third parties that exceeds the threshold for personal moral sanction.

Where critics of non-users sometimes have a narrower point is in cases of active hypocrisy — the person who makes loud public moral claims about AI use while privately using AI, or who attacks others for accessibility choices they themselves have not examined. That kind of specific inconsistency is a legitimate target for specific criticism. What is not legitimate is treating "refusal to use AI" as itself a moral failing. The autonomy defence runs in both directions, and that is what makes the essay's position pro-autonomy rather than pro-AI. The user who uses AI quietly and the non-user who refuses AI quietly are both within the neutral default.

7. Where Neutrality Actually Weakens: The Edge Cases

A rigorous position has to name its limits. The neutrality claim is strongest for non-commercial personal use and weakens along a spectrum as the use case approaches specific forms of commercial and targeted activity. These edge cases are worth naming explicitly, both because they are where the strongest critics have genuine ground, and because specifying them makes the core claim more defensible, not less.

The clearest edge case is targeted style imitation of a specific identifiable living artist. Someone who trains a LoRA on a single named artist's portfolio and sells output in that artist's style is not making a diffuse marginal contribution to a system; they are directing a specific harm at a specific person. The 2022 Mimic launch, shut down within 24 hours under pressure, represented this case in stark form: the tool could replicate individual styles from ten sample images. Even here, the appropriate response is civil society's normal tools — naming the specific act, platform pressure to de-list offending models, professional consequences, support for the affected artist, legal remedies where applicable. Not harassment campaigns directed at the perpetrator, and certainly not harassment of the broader user base. But the neutrality claim does genuinely weaken here, and pretending otherwise would be dishonest.

A secondary edge case is commercial work sold into markets with known living competitors, especially when the seller is ambiguous about AI involvement. The harm here is more diffuse than targeted imitation, but the commercial dimension changes the calculus from "personal expression" to "economic activity in a contested market," and disclosure norms (when the buyer is relying on a perceived human-made good) do plausibly apply. This is the same kind of question mass-produced-versus-artisanal goods have always faced, and the same answer applies: disclosure is reasonable, outright prohibition is not, and the moral weight is not on the individual producer so much as on the market-level transparency norms.

A third case, much narrower: use patterns that demonstrably harm the user themselves — the person substituting AI for every act of sustained attention, using it in ways that track their own documented decline in the skills they valued. This is a personal-discernment case, not a third-party-harm case, and it does not license outside moral judgement. But it is a reason the individual might want to examine their own patterns.

Acknowledging these edge cases does not undermine the core neutrality claim. It strengthens it. The claim is not "all AI use is automatically neutral regardless of what the user does." The claim is "the individual choice to use or refuse AI is neutral in the default case, with specific narrow cases that require specific proportional responses — none of which is harassment of users as a category."

8. Why Harassment Is Always the Wrong Response

Even in the edge cases where moral weight genuinely attaches — the targeted style imitation, the undisclosed commercial substitution — harassment remains the wrong response, both ethically and practically. This is a narrower and stronger claim than the neutrality claim, and it holds across virtually all the cases the neutrality claim does not.

Ethically, proportionality constrains moral response. Political scientist Rochelle Terman's research on public shaming — forthcoming as Backlash: Defiance, Human Rights, and the Politics of Shame — finds that shaming is consistently most effective within closed networks with shared norms and reputational stakes, and consistently backfires when wielded by cultural outsiders against targets who see the sanctioned behaviour as benign. Research on online shaming specifically shows high rates of collateral damage, including false accusations: the 2022 Ben Moran case, where an illustrator was banned from r/Art after moderators wrongly concluded his 100-hour documented piece was AI-generated, is one of many documented instances where harassment campaigns have hit human artists whose style approximated "AI tells." The sanction consistently misses its nominal target.

Practically, harassment misdirects attention and energy away from the targets where pressure can actually move outcomes. Northwestern management professor Brayden King's research on corporate boycotts finds that the strongest predictor of effectiveness is media attention directed at a specific high-profile corporate target — not volume of individual-consumer action. The movements that have meaningfully shifted corporate behaviour (anti-apartheid divestment, Brent Spar) directed pressure institutionally. Movements that diffuse pressure across individual consumers dilute their own impact. The anti-AI movement's decision to attack users rather than developers is both ethically disproportionate and strategically counterproductive: every unit of energy spent harassing a random Midjourney user is a unit not spent on OpenAI, Stability AI, Midjourney Inc., Meta, or Google.

This part of the argument is not about neutrality — it is a standalone claim that survives even if someone rejects the neutrality analysis entirely. Even if you believe individual AI users are morally culpable for participating in a harmful system, harassment is still a disproportionate and ineffective response. The argument has two layers: the primary claim that use is neutral in the default case, and the secondary claim that harassment is wrong even where neutrality might be contested. Both layers support the same practical conclusion.

The same analysis runs symmetrically for harassment of non-users, though the case arises less often. Someone who dismisses a colleague as "a Luddite" for refusing to use AI, who mocks artists for choosing traditional media, or who treats refusal as a personal insult is doing the same structural thing — attaching disproportionate moral weight to someone else's autonomous technology choice. It is less common in the current moment because the cultural energy flows the other way, but the principle is the same. Autonomy runs both directions.

9. What This Essay Is Not Claiming

Because pro-autonomy positions are routinely misread as pro-AI positions, it is worth marking explicitly what this essay does not claim. It does not claim AI is good, aesthetically valuable, economically benign, or environmentally unproblematic. It does not claim the consent issues in training data are resolved. It does not claim displaced workers are not owed support. It does not claim human creative practice is unaffected by widespread AI adoption. It does not claim anyone should use AI, or that refusing it is irrational. It does not claim the corporations running AI companies are acting ethically.

All of these positions can be held, with full rigour, alongside the essay's actual claim: that the individual choice to use or refuse AI sits within the default zone of morally neutral personal autonomy, and that the serious harm arguments advanced against either choice do not, on examination, succeed in moving that default. This is a narrow claim, specific in scope, tested against the strongest versions of the counter-arguments, and consistent with a wide range of views about AI as a technology and industry. A reader who believes AI is an ecological disaster, a labour catastrophe, an aesthetic regression, and a corporate consent violation all at once can still agree that the individual user choice is not the appropriate target of that concern — and that pressure belongs at the corporate, regulatory, and cultural-design levels where it can actually move outcomes.

10. The Verdict

The default for technology-adoption choices is moral neutrality. That default can be moved, but only by harm arguments that actually attribute the harm to the individual choice in a way that survives proportionality analysis. For AI, the serious arguments — complicity in training-data extraction, contribution to labour displacement, degradation of human creative practice — each identify a real harm and then fail at the attribution step. The harms are real; their path runs through corporate actors, platform operators, regulatory regimes, and cultural infrastructure, not through the individual user's marginal choice. Directing moral weight at individual users mis-specifies both the cause and the effective response.

This is not pro-AI. The corporate practices that scraped training data without consent are a genuine ethical problem and they warrant regulation, litigation, and pressure. Labour displacement is real and warrants collective bargaining, just-transition policy, and industrial protection. Effects on human cognitive practice are worth monitoring and discussing at the level of education and cultural design. These are serious questions and they are not resolved by the essay. What the essay resolves is the narrower question of whether the individual user deserves moral judgement for their adoption choice. They do not. Neither does the non-user. Both sit within the default zone of autonomous consumer choice that protects most of ordinary life from being transformed into a constant moral performance.

A thoughtful person can dislike AI output, refuse to consume it, argue publicly against AI development, support displaced workers financially, and advocate for regulation — while simultaneously declining to judge individual users for their choices. A thoughtful person can also use AI casually for personal creative or productive work, recognise its genuine usefulness, and enjoy the outputs — while declining to judge non-users for their refusal. These positions are compatible, and they are what a consistent application of the default of neutrality looks like. The only incompatible position is the one that weaponises moral judgement at the individual level in either direction. That position does not survive examination, and is what this essay argues against.
