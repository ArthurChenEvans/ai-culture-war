A technical walkthrough of how large language models actually work — from next-word prediction and transformer architecture to fact storage and superposition — followed by the practitioner's view from the programming trenches. Not magic. Not theft. Mathematics.

1. The Core Idea: Predicting the Next Word

Grant Sanderson, the mathematician behind 3Blue1Brown, opens his explanation of large language models with an analogy that cuts through the noise immediately (3Blue1Brown, 2024a). Imagine you find a movie script describing a conversation between a person and their AI assistant, but the AI's response has been torn off. If you had a machine that could look at any piece of text and sensibly predict what word comes next, you could reconstruct the missing dialogue one word at a time — feeding in what you have, reading off the prediction, appending it, and repeating. When you interact with a chatbot, this is exactly what is happening. A large language model is, at its core, a sophisticated mathematical function that takes a piece of text and assigns a probability to every possible next word (3Blue1Brown, 2024a).

This is the first and most important thing to understand about the AI culture war: the technology at the centre of the debate is not a sentient mind, not a creative agent, and not a thief. It is a probability engine. It takes text in and produces a weighted list of what might come next. To build a chatbot from this engine, you lay out some introductory text describing a helpful AI assistant, append whatever the user has typed, and then let the model repeatedly predict what such an assistant would say next, one word at a time (3Blue1Brown, 2024a). The output is then presented to the user as a response.

Crucially, the model does not predict one word with certainty. It assigns probabilities across all possible next words. The output tends to look more natural if it is allowed to occasionally select less likely words at random, which means that even though the model itself is a deterministic mathematical function, the same prompt will typically produce a different answer each time it is run (3Blue1Brown, 2024a). This is not evidence of creativity or consciousness. It is a deliberate design feature — randomness injected into a probability distribution to avoid repetitive, stilted output.

The distinction matters enormously for the culture war. When someone claims that AI "understands" or "creates" or "steals ideas," they are making a philosophical claim about a machine that does one thing: predict the statistically most plausible next word. Whether that prediction constitutes understanding is a separate question — one explored in the Philosophical Explanation — but the mechanism itself is pure mathematics.

2. Training: Tuning 175 Billion Dials

How does the model learn to make these predictions? By processing an enormous amount of text, typically pulled from the internet. Sanderson puts the scale into perspective: for a human to read the amount of text used to train GPT-3, reading non-stop 24 hours a day, seven days a week, it would take over 2,600 years. Larger models since then have trained on far, far more (3Blue1Brown, 2024a).

The way a language model behaves is entirely determined by continuous numerical values called parameters or weights. GPT-3 has 175 billion of them (3Blue1Brown, 2024b). No human ever deliberately sets those parameters. They begin at random — meaning the model initially outputs gibberish — and are then repeatedly refined based on example text. The training process works by passing all but the last word of an example into the model, comparing the model's prediction with the true last word, and then using an algorithm called backpropagation to tweak all 175 billion parameters so that the model becomes slightly more likely to choose the correct word next time (3Blue1Brown, 2024a).

The scale of computation involved is staggering. Sanderson poses the question directly: if you could perform one billion additions and multiplications every single second, how long would it take to do all the operations involved in training the largest language models? A year? Ten thousand years? The answer is well over 100 million years (3Blue1Brown, 2024a). This astronomical computation is only made possible by GPUs — specialised computer chips optimised for running many operations in parallel.

But raw pre-training is only part of the story. The goal of auto-completing a random passage of internet text is very different from the goal of being a helpful AI assistant. To bridge this gap, chatbots undergo a second phase of training called reinforcement learning with human feedback (RLHF). Human workers flag unhelpful or problematic outputs, and their corrections further adjust the model's parameters to make it more likely to produce responses that users actually prefer (3Blue1Brown, 2024a). This is how a next-word predictor becomes something that feels like a conversational partner.

3. The Transformer: How Words Talk to Each Other

Prior to 2017, most language models processed text one word at a time — sequentially, from left to right. This made them slow and difficult to parallelise. Then a team of researchers at Google introduced a new architecture called the transformer, and everything changed. Transformers do not read text from start to finish; they process it all at once, in parallel (3Blue1Brown, 2024a). This is the architecture that put the "T" in GPT.

Sanderson's deeper technical series (3Blue1Brown, 2024b) walks through the transformer step by step. The very first thing that happens is that each word in the input text is converted into a vector — a long list of numbers. In GPT-3, each word becomes a list of 12,288 numbers (3Blue1Brown, 2024b). These vectors are not arbitrary; during training, the model learns to position words so that similar meanings correspond to nearby vectors in this high-dimensional space. The classic example: if you take the vector for "woman," subtract the vector for "man," and add the result to "uncle," you land very close to "aunt" (3Blue1Brown, 2024b; 3Blue1Brown, 2024c). The direction between "man" and "woman" encodes gender information, and the model has learned this structure entirely from data.

The sequence of word vectors then flows through two types of operation, repeated many times. The first is attention — the signature innovation of the transformer. Attention allows the vectors to "talk to each other," passing information back and forth so that each vector can update its meaning based on the words around it. The word "model" in the phrase "a machine learning model" is different from "model" in "a fashion model." The attention mechanism is what figures out which surrounding words are relevant to updating the meaning of which other words, and exactly how those meanings should change (3Blue1Brown, 2024b). Critically, all of this happens in parallel, not sequentially.

The second operation is the multi-layer perceptron (MLP), sometimes called the feed-forward layer. Unlike attention, the vectors do not talk to each other here — each one goes through the same set of operations independently. The MLP can be thought of as asking a long list of questions about each vector and updating it based on the answers (3Blue1Brown, 2024b). Where attention is responsible for incorporating context, the MLP is where the model stores general knowledge — facts it learned during training.

In GPT-3, the data flows through 96 iterations of these two blocks. By the end, the hope is that the final vector in the sequence has absorbed enough information — both from the surrounding context and from the knowledge baked into the model's weights — to produce an accurate prediction of what word comes next (3Blue1Brown, 2024b). A final matrix called the unembedding matrix converts this last vector into a probability distribution over all 50,257 possible tokens in the vocabulary. The result is then passed through a softmax function, which normalises the raw scores into valid probabilities (3Blue1Brown, 2024b).

4. Where Do Facts Live? The Michael Jordan Problem

If you feed a large language model the prompt "Michael Jordan plays the sport of ___" and it correctly predicts "basketball," then somewhere inside its hundreds of billions of parameters, the model has baked in a specific fact about a specific person. Where does that fact live? Sanderson's chapter on this question (3Blue1Brown, 2024c) draws on research from Google DeepMind to provide a partial answer: facts appear to be stored primarily inside the multi-layer perceptrons.

The mechanism works through a sequence of matrix multiplications with a non-linear function sandwiched in between. The first matrix — the "up projection" — takes the vector being processed and multiplies it by a very large matrix whose rows can be thought of as questions. Each row computes a dot product with the input vector, effectively asking "how much does this vector align with a particular direction in the embedding space?" For example, one row might encode the direction "first name Michael + last name Jordan." The dot product with that row will be high if and only if the vector represents the full name Michael Jordan, and low otherwise (3Blue1Brown, 2024c).

The result then passes through a non-linear function called ReLU — the rectified linear unit — which simply clips all negative values to zero and leaves positive values unchanged. This acts as an AND gate: the output is positive only when both conditions (first name Michael AND last name Jordan) are met simultaneously. Without this non-linearity, a high score for "Michael Phelps" or "Alexis Jordan" would also partially trigger the response, because a purely linear operation cannot distinguish between partial and full matches (3Blue1Brown, 2024c).

The second matrix — the "down projection" — then takes the result and maps it back to the embedding space. Its columns determine what gets added to the vector when a particular neuron is active. If the first column encodes the direction "basketball," then whenever the Michael Jordan neuron fires, the basketball direction gets added to the output. The model has thereby stored and retrieved a specific fact: Michael Jordan → basketball (3Blue1Brown, 2024c). In GPT-3, each MLP block contains just under 50,000 of these neurons, and there are 96 such blocks, giving the MLPs a total of approximately 116 billion parameters — roughly two-thirds of the entire network (3Blue1Brown, 2024c).

This is a toy example, and Sanderson is explicit about that. Real neurons rarely represent a single clean feature like "Michael Jordan." But the mathematics of the toy example — dot products as pattern detectors, ReLU as a gate, column vectors as stored associations — is real. The computation is exactly what happens inside every MLP block. The question is only how many features each neuron is actually encoding simultaneously.

5. Superposition: Why the Model Knows More Than It "Should"

This brings us to perhaps the most thought-provoking mathematical insight in the entire 3Blue1Brown series: the phenomenon of superposition. If you have an n-dimensional space and you want to represent independent features using perpendicular directions — so that adding one feature does not interfere with any other — then the maximum number of features you can represent is exactly n. This is, in fact, the mathematical definition of dimension (3Blue1Brown, 2024c).

But what happens if you relax the constraint slightly and allow features to be represented by directions that are nearly perpendicular — say, between 89 and 91 degrees apart? In two or three dimensions, this makes almost no difference. But in high-dimensional spaces, the answer changes dramatically. Sanderson demonstrates this with a simple Python experiment: in a 100-dimensional space, you can cram 10,000 nearly perpendicular vectors — 100 times more than the number of dimensions — such that all pairwise angles fall between 89 and 91 degrees (3Blue1Brown, 2024c).

This result is a consequence of the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma: the number of nearly perpendicular vectors you can fit into a space grows exponentially with the number of dimensions. For large language models, this has a profound implication. A model operating in a 12,288-dimensional space is not limited to storing 12,288 independent features. It can potentially store an astronomically larger number of features by using nearly perpendicular directions, tolerating a small amount of noise in exchange for massive storage capacity (3Blue1Brown, 2024c).

This is the hypothesis of superposition: individual features are not represented by single neurons lighting up, but by specific combinations of neurons — patterns spread across the network. This may partially explain why model performance seems to scale so well with size: a space with 10 times as many dimensions can store far, far more than 10 times as many independent ideas (3Blue1Brown, 2024c). It also explains why these models are so difficult to interpret — the features are superimposed, overlapping, entangled. Researchers in the field of mechanistic interpretability use tools called sparse autoencoders to try to disentangle these superimposed features, and Anthropic (the company behind Claude) has published extensive research on this problem (3Blue1Brown, 2024c).

Superposition is significant for the culture war because it undermines the common critique that AI merely "memorises and regurgitates." If the model were simply storing training data in retrievable form, you would expect a one-to-one mapping between neurons and facts. Instead, the mathematical evidence suggests that the model compresses and distributes knowledge across overlapping patterns in a way that is qualitatively different from database storage — and qualitatively different from simple copying.

6. Temperature and the Illusion of Creativity

One of the most misunderstood features of LLMs is what practitioners call "temperature." After the model produces its probability distribution over possible next words, the softmax function that normalises those probabilities includes an adjustable constant T — the temperature — in the denominator of its exponents (3Blue1Brown, 2024b). When T is low (approaching zero), the model always picks the single most probable word, producing predictable, formulaic output. When T is high, it gives more weight to less likely options, producing output that feels more "creative" but risks degenerating into nonsense.

Sanderson demonstrates this directly by generating stories from GPT-3 at different temperature settings. At temperature zero, the model produces a derivative version of Goldilocks — maximally predictable. At higher temperatures, the output begins more originally but quickly loses coherence (3Blue1Brown, 2024b). This is not creativity in any human sense. It is a dial that controls how far from the statistical centre the random sampler is allowed to wander. The "creativity" of a language model is, mechanically, the degree to which it is permitted to select improbable words.

This matters for the cultural debate because the outputs that people find most impressive — the surprisingly apt metaphor, the unexpected turn of phrase, the seemingly insightful connection — are often the result of high-temperature sampling landing on a low-probability but contextually coherent word. The model did not "think" of a clever metaphor. It rolled a dice weighted by statistical patterns in its training data, and the dice happened to land on something that reads as clever to a human audience. The mechanism is elegant, but it is not thought.

7. The Practitioner's View: Linus Torvalds and ThePrimeagen

Understanding the mechanism is one thing. Living with it as a professional tool is another. In a widely discussed conversation, Linus Torvalds — the creator of Linux and one of the most influential software engineers alive — was asked whether modern generative AI is a bubble, a computing revolution, or both. His answer was immediate: it is clearly both (Torvalds, 2025, as discussed in ThePrimeagen, 2025). ThePrimeagen, a popular programming YouTuber and former Netflix engineer, reacted with frustration at what he saw as fence-sitting, pointing out an apparent contradiction: Torvalds simultaneously said AI would change how most skilled jobs get done, and also that it is not as revolutionary as people make it out to be.

But this is not a contradiction — it is the mechanical reality playing out in professional practice. Torvalds' position maps directly onto the technical architecture described above. A next-word predictor trained on the internet's entire corpus of code will be extraordinarily good at predicting the next line of code in a given context. It will change how programmers work. But it is still a next-word predictor. It does not understand system architecture, it does not reason about trade-offs across a codebase, and it does not maintain a coherent design vision over thousands of lines. The revolution is real; the limits are also real. Holding both truths simultaneously is not fence-sitting — it is engineering literacy.

Torvalds made this point sharply when discussing "vibe coding" — the practice of letting AI generate large amounts of code with minimal human oversight. His assessment: AI is great for getting into programming, but it is going to be a horrible thing to maintain (Torvalds, 2025, via ThePrimeagen, 2025). ThePrimeagen expanded on this with a devastating anecdote from a colleague whose team had been required to vibe-code at speed. Every individual pull request looked correct. Every small decision by the AI was functionally good. The code at each tiny step appeared right. But when the team zoomed out and looked at the system as a whole, the result was, in the colleague's words, the worst code he had ever seen in his life (ThePrimeagen, 2025).

This is the mechanical explanation made flesh. A next-word predictor is optimised for local coherence — the next token, the next line, the next function. It has no mechanism for global coherence — the overall system design, the architectural trade-offs, the long-term maintainability. Each prediction is locally correct and globally blind. This is not a bug in the implementation. It is a direct consequence of what the model fundamentally is: a function that predicts the next word.

8. The Dichotomy of Two Worlds

ThePrimeagen raises a cultural observation that maps onto the mechanical reality in a striking way: the artist community is furious about AI, while the programming community has largely embraced it. The same technology, built on the same mechanism, trained on the same kind of data — other people's work, scraped from the internet, synthesised, and sold back to users — provokes opposite reactions in two creative professions (ThePrimeagen, 2025). ThePrimeagen finds this deeply strange, and challenges the common explanation that "artistry requires more originality" than programming.

The mechanical explanation offers a lens for understanding this dichotomy. In programming, the output of an LLM is testable. Code either compiles or it does not. It either passes the test suite or it does not. The programmer can verify the prediction, reject it if it is wrong, and use it as a productivity accelerator when it is right. The next-word predictor is being used as a tool — one that the practitioner controls, evaluates, and overrides. In art, the output is the final product. There is no compiler, no test suite, no objective verification step. When an AI generates an image or a piece of music, the prediction is the work. The artist is not accelerated; the artist is replaced. The mechanism is identical, but the relationship between the prediction and the professional is fundamentally different.

ThePrimeagen also highlights the chess analogy, crediting it as one of the best points made in the discussion. Chess has never been more computationally solved than it is today — a phone app can beat Magnus Carlsen — yet chess has never been more popular. The median player may be irrelevant against an engine, but the best human players are more celebrated, more watched, and more valued than ever (ThePrimeagen, 2025). The prediction: the same will happen with creative professions. The average photographer will be pushed out of business by AI-generated imagery. But the truly exceptional photographers — the ones with a genuine eye for capturing a moment — will become more valuable, because audiences saturated with AI slop will hunger for human ingenuity and the authenticity of a real human vision.

9. The Bubble, the Crash, and the Genie

Torvalds' final observation is perhaps the most important for the broader culture war. He describes himself as a huge believer in AI as a technology, but not a believer in the market and marketing surrounding it. He predicts that there will be a crash, and that it will be ugly (Torvalds, 2025, via ThePrimeagen, 2025). This directly echoes the environmental essay's distinction between the technology and the industry: the next-word predictor is genuinely useful; the venture-capital-fuelled gold rush around it is a bubble. Both things are true simultaneously.

On the question of training data — whether it constitutes theft to train a model on publicly available code and art — Torvalds' response is blunt: "That's reality. Deal with it. That genie is out of the bottle. You're not getting it back" (Torvalds, 2025, via ThePrimeagen, 2025). ThePrimeagen notes that this position, while pragmatically accurate, is also one that the art community would find outrageous. The question of whether scraping and synthesising other people's work constitutes theft is explored fully in the Legal and Ethical Explanations. But Torvalds' point is a mechanical one, not a moral one: the models exist, the capability is real, and no policy intervention will uninvent the transformer architecture.

ThePrimeagen's own prediction for the near future is characteristically direct. For the next ten years, while AI transitions from infancy into something reliable, the biggest visible impact will be an explosion of low-quality, AI-generated content — "hand-crafted, free-range, beautiful, absolute horseshit content at all times everywhere" (ThePrimeagen, 2025). The mechanism explains why: a next-word predictor trained on the internet's median content will, by definition, produce median content. It will be fluent, it will be grammatically correct, and it will be statistically plausible. But it will also be derivative, undifferentiated, and architecturally incoherent at scale — exactly what Torvalds warned about with vibe coding.

10. The Verdict: A Machine That Predicts, Not a Mind That Thinks

The anti-AI camp wants to say: "It's just autocomplete. It's stupid. It can't do anything." They are wrong about the capability — the mathematics of attention, superposition, and high-dimensional embedding spaces produces results that are genuinely useful, often startlingly so. The pro-AI camp wants to say: "It understands. It creates. It reasons." They are wrong about the mechanism — it predicts the next word, stores facts as matrix products, and generates "creativity" by rolling weighted dice.

By understanding the mechanism, we arrive at the only honest position: these models are extraordinarily powerful tools that are fundamentally limited by what they are. They excel at local prediction — the next word, the next line, the next plausible continuation. They fail at global coherence — sustained reasoning, architectural vision, long-term consistency. They compress knowledge through superposition in ways that are qualitatively different from either human memory or database storage. And they produce outputs whose apparent creativity is a mathematical artefact of temperature-controlled sampling from a probability distribution.

Three concurrent realities must be acknowledged:

Mechanically, a large language model is a transformer — a stack of attention blocks and MLPs, containing hundreds of billions of learned parameters, that converts input text into a probability distribution over possible next words. The parameters encode compressed statistical patterns from the training data, stored via superposition in high-dimensional vector spaces. The entire process is matrix multiplication, non-linear activation functions, and softmax normalisation. It is mathematics, not magic.

Practically, the tool is genuinely transformative for professionals who understand its limits. Torvalds and ThePrimeagen both agree: AI will make people more productive, it is great for small-scale code prediction, and it is a disaster when used without human oversight at system-level scale. The chess analogy holds — the median practitioner is threatened, but the best practitioners will become more valuable as audiences learn to distinguish human ingenuity from statistical median.

Culturally, the market surrounding AI is a bubble, and the bubble will crash. The technology will survive the crash because the transformer architecture is real and the mathematics works. But the hype, the valuations, the breathless promises of imminent artificial general intelligence — all of that is marketing built on top of a next-word predictor. Understanding the mechanism is the vaccine against both irrational fear and irrational hype.
