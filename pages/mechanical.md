# The AI Culture War — Mechanical Explanation

**3Blue1Brown · Torvalds · Attention · Superposition · Memorisation**

## The Mechanical Explanation

A technical walkthrough of how large language models actually work — from next-word prediction and transformer architecture to fact storage, superposition, and the empirically-documented memorisation that complicates the "it's not copying" defence — followed by the practitioner's view from the programming trenches. Not magic. Not clean. Mathematics with edge cases.

## 1. The Core Idea: Predicting the Next Word

Grant Sanderson, the mathematician behind 3Blue1Brown, opens his explanation of large language models with an analogy that cuts through the noise immediately (3Blue1Brown, 2024a). Imagine you find a movie script describing a conversation between a person and their AI assistant, but the AI's response has been torn off. If you had a machine that could look at any piece of text and sensibly predict what word comes next, you could reconstruct the missing dialogue one word at a time — feeding in what you have, reading off the prediction, appending it, and repeating. When you interact with a chatbot, this is exactly what is happening. A large language model is, at its core, a sophisticated mathematical function that takes a piece of text and assigns a probability to every possible next word (3Blue1Brown, 2024a).

This is the first and most important thing to understand about the AI culture war: the technology at the centre of the debate is not a sentient mind, not a creative agent, and not — as some descriptions suggest — a simple collage machine. It is a probability engine. It takes text in and produces a weighted list of what might come next. To build a chatbot from this engine, you lay out some introductory text describing a helpful AI assistant, append whatever the user has typed, and then let the model repeatedly predict what such an assistant would say next, one word at a time (3Blue1Brown, 2024a). The output is then presented to the user as a response.

Crucially, the model does not predict one word with certainty. It assigns probabilities across all possible next words. The output tends to look more natural if it is allowed to occasionally select less likely words at random, which means that even though the model itself is a deterministic mathematical function, the same prompt will typically produce a different answer each time it is run (3Blue1Brown, 2024a). This is not evidence of creativity or consciousness. It is a deliberate design feature — randomness injected into a probability distribution to avoid repetitive, stilted output.

> The distinction matters enormously for the culture war. When someone claims that AI "understands" or "creates" or "steals ideas," they are making a philosophical claim about a machine that does one thing: predict the statistically most plausible next word. Whether that prediction constitutes understanding is a separate question — one explored in the Philosophical Explanation — but the mechanism itself is pure mathematics.

## 2. Training: Tuning 175 Billion Dials

How does the model learn to make these predictions? By processing an enormous amount of text, typically pulled from the internet. Sanderson puts the scale into perspective: for a human to read the amount of text used to train GPT-3, reading non-stop 24 hours a day, seven days a week, it would take over 2,600 years. Larger models since then have trained on far, far more (3Blue1Brown, 2024a).

The way a language model behaves is entirely determined by continuous numerical values called parameters or weights. GPT-3 has 175 billion of them (3Blue1Brown, 2024b). No human ever deliberately sets those parameters. They begin at random — meaning the model initially outputs gibberish — and are then repeatedly refined based on example text. The training process works by passing all but the last word of an example into the model, comparing the model's prediction with the true last word, and then using an algorithm called backpropagation to tweak all 175 billion parameters so that the model becomes slightly more likely to choose the correct word next time (3Blue1Brown, 2024a).

The scale of computation involved is extraordinary. Published estimates put the total compute used to train GPT-3 at roughly 3.14 × 10²³ floating-point operations. At a rate of one billion operations per second, that would take on the order of ten million years — a figure Sanderson gives as "well over 100 million years" in some framings depending on which operations are counted (3Blue1Brown, 2024a). Either way, the point stands: this computation is only made possible by GPUs — specialised computer chips optimised for running many operations in parallel — and by cluster-scale infrastructure that did not exist a decade ago.

But raw pre-training is only part of the story. The goal of auto-completing a random passage of internet text is very different from the goal of being a helpful AI assistant. To bridge this gap, chatbots undergo a second phase of training called reinforcement learning with human feedback (RLHF). Human workers flag unhelpful or problematic outputs, and their corrections further adjust the model's parameters to make it more likely to produce responses that users actually prefer (3Blue1Brown, 2024a). This is how a next-word predictor becomes something that feels like a conversational partner.

## 3. The Transformer: How Words Talk to Each Other

Prior to 2017, most language models processed text one word at a time — sequentially, from left to right. This made them slow and difficult to parallelise. Then a team of researchers at Google introduced a new architecture called the transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017), and everything changed. Transformers do not read text from start to finish; they process it all at once, in parallel (3Blue1Brown, 2024a). This is the architecture that put the "T" in GPT.

Sanderson's deeper technical series (3Blue1Brown, 2024b) walks through the transformer step by step. The very first thing that happens is that each word in the input text is converted into a vector — a long list of numbers. In GPT-3, each word becomes a list of 12,288 numbers (3Blue1Brown, 2024b). These vectors are not arbitrary; during training, the model learns to position words so that similar meanings correspond to nearby vectors in this high-dimensional space. The classic example: if you take the vector for "woman," subtract the vector for "man," and add the result to "uncle," you land very close to "aunt" (3Blue1Brown, 2024b; 3Blue1Brown, 2024c). The direction between "man" and "woman" encodes gender information, and the model has learned this structure entirely from data.

The core mechanism that allows the transformer to work is called *attention*. At each layer, every word's vector can "look at" every other word's vector and update itself based on what is relevant. The word "bank" in "river bank" and "bank account" starts with the same initial vector, but after several attention layers, the two vectors have diverged — the first has absorbed context from "river," the second from "account." The final vector for the last word in the input is what gets used to predict the next word (3Blue1Brown, 2024b). This is how a system that fundamentally processes tokens can appear to handle semantics: the meaning emerges from the learned pattern of which vectors attend to which other vectors.

## 4. Fact Storage: Facts as Matrix Products

Where, physically, does a language model "store" the fact that Michael Jordan plays basketball? Sanderson's third technical chapter (3Blue1Brown, 2024c) addresses this with remarkable clarity. The answer is not a lookup table. Facts are stored in the multi-layer perceptron (MLP) blocks between attention layers — specifically, as patterns of weights in two large matrices that, when multiplied against an appropriate input vector, produce an output that encodes the fact. The "Michael Jordan" direction in vector space combines with the weights to yield a vector that has been nudged toward "basketball." The fact is a learned geometric relationship, not a stored string.

This distributed storage has several consequences. It makes models extraordinarily parameter-efficient for common facts that show up repeatedly in training data. It also makes them prone to confident errors — the geometry that almost encodes a fact may, under slightly different input conditions, encode a similar-sounding but wrong fact. Hallucination is not a bug in the implementation; it is a natural consequence of storing information as learned weights rather than as retrievable entries.

## 5. Superposition: Why the Model Knows More Than It "Should"

This brings us to perhaps the most thought-provoking mathematical insight in the 3Blue1Brown series: the phenomenon of superposition. If you have an n-dimensional space and you want to represent independent features using perpendicular directions — so that adding one feature does not interfere with any other — then the maximum number of features you can represent is exactly n. This is, in fact, the mathematical definition of dimension (3Blue1Brown, 2024c).

But what happens if you relax the constraint slightly and allow features to be represented by directions that are *nearly* perpendicular — say, between 89 and 91 degrees apart? In two or three dimensions, this makes almost no difference. But in high-dimensional spaces, the answer changes dramatically. Sanderson demonstrates this with a simple Python experiment: in a 100-dimensional space, you can cram 10,000 nearly perpendicular vectors — 100 times more than the number of dimensions — such that all pairwise angles fall between 89 and 91 degrees (3Blue1Brown, 2024c).

This result is a consequence of the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma: the number of nearly perpendicular vectors you can fit into a space grows **exponentially** with the number of dimensions. For large language models, this has a profound implication. A model operating in a 12,288-dimensional space is not limited to storing 12,288 independent features. It can potentially store an astronomically larger number of features by using nearly perpendicular directions, tolerating a small amount of noise in exchange for massive storage capacity (3Blue1Brown, 2024c).

This is the hypothesis of superposition: individual features are not represented by single neurons lighting up, but by specific combinations of neurons — patterns spread across the network. This may partially explain why model performance seems to scale so well with size: a space with 10 times as many dimensions can store far more than 10 times as many independent ideas. It also explains why these models are so difficult to interpret — the features are superimposed, overlapping, entangled. Researchers in the field of mechanistic interpretability use tools called sparse autoencoders to try to disentangle these superimposed features, and Anthropic (the company behind Claude) has published extensive research on this problem (3Blue1Brown, 2024c; Bricken et al., 2023).

> Superposition is relevant to the culture war, but the relevance needs to be stated carefully. The common framing that diffusion and language models are "just a collage of stolen images" or "just copying" does not match the mechanism — the storage is distributed, overlapping, and geometric, not pixel-for-pixel. But superposition *does not* mean the model has fully abstracted away from training data. Empirical research, most notably Carlini et al. (2023) on Stable Diffusion, has shown that models can and do reproduce training images in recognisable form, especially for images that appear many times in the training set. Nicholas Carlini and colleagues extracted over a thousand near-verbatim training images from Stable Diffusion using targeted prompts. The mathematics of superposition reduces how much of any given training example is retrievable from the model, but it does not eliminate it. An honest mechanical account acknowledges both: the model is not a simple collage machine *and* the model demonstrably memorises specific training examples under some conditions. The legal implications of this are covered in the Legal Explanation.

## 6. Temperature and the Illusion of Creativity

One of the most misunderstood features of LLMs is what practitioners call "temperature." After the model produces its probability distribution over possible next words, the softmax function that normalises those probabilities includes an adjustable constant T — the temperature — in the denominator of its exponents (3Blue1Brown, 2024b). When T is low (approaching zero), the model always picks the single most probable word, producing predictable, formulaic output. When T is high, it gives more weight to less likely options, producing output that feels more "creative" but risks degenerating into nonsense.

Sanderson demonstrates this directly by generating stories from GPT-3 at different temperature settings. At temperature zero, the model produces a derivative version of Goldilocks — maximally predictable. At higher temperatures, the output begins more originally but quickly loses coherence (3Blue1Brown, 2024b). This is not creativity in any human sense. It is a dial that controls how far from the statistical centre the random sampler is allowed to wander. The "creativity" of a language model is, mechanically, the degree to which it is permitted to select improbable words.

This matters for the cultural debate because the outputs that people find most impressive — the surprisingly apt metaphor, the unexpected turn of phrase, the seemingly insightful connection — are often the result of high-temperature sampling landing on a low-probability but contextually coherent word. The model did not "think" of a clever metaphor. It rolled a dice weighted by statistical patterns in its training data, and the dice happened to land on something that reads as clever to a human audience. The mechanism is elegant, but it is not thought in the way humans mean the word.

## 7. The Practitioner's View: Linus Torvalds at Open Source Summit

Understanding the mechanism is one thing. Living with it as a professional tool is another. At the Linux Foundation Open Source Summit in Seoul in November 2025, Linus Torvalds — the creator of Linux and Git, and one of the most influential software engineers alive — was interviewed by Dirk Hohndel, head of open source at Verizon. Asked whether AI is a bubble, a computing revolution, or both, Torvalds answered directly: it is clearly both (Torvalds, 2025). Pressed to reconcile the apparent tension, he elaborated that AI will change how most skilled jobs get done, while not being as revolutionary as the marketing around it suggests.

This is not a contradiction — it is the mechanical reality playing out in professional practice. A next-word predictor trained on the internet's entire corpus of code *will* be extraordinarily good at predicting the next line of code in a given context. It *will* change how programmers work. But it is still a next-word predictor. It does not understand system architecture, it does not reason about trade-offs across a codebase, and it does not maintain a coherent design vision over thousands of lines. Holding both truths simultaneously — the revolution is real, the limits are also real — is not fence-sitting. It is engineering literacy.

Torvalds made a sharp distinction when discussing "vibe coding" — the practice of letting AI generate large amounts of code with minimal human oversight. He described himself as "fairly positive" about vibe coding as an on-ramp into programming for people who otherwise could not write code, while warning it would be "a horrible, horrible idea from a maintenance standpoint" for production work (Torvalds, 2025, via Open Source Summit interview). The distinction maps directly onto the mechanical architecture: local prediction is genuinely good, global coherence is genuinely absent, and pretending otherwise is a recipe for code that is locally correct and globally unmaintainable.

> This is the mechanical explanation made flesh. A next-word predictor is optimised for local coherence — the next token, the next line, the next function. It has no architectural mechanism for global coherence — the overall system design, the architectural trade-offs, the long-term maintainability. Each prediction is locally correct and globally blind. This is not a bug in the implementation. It is a direct consequence of what the model fundamentally is: a function that predicts the next word.

A January 2026 epilogue is worth recording honestly. Torvalds quietly began using AI-assisted coding — specifically Google's Antigravity — for a personal audio-processing project (AudioNoise), and openly credited the tool in the commit description. Commentators across the software industry noted this as a significant pivot from his earlier public scepticism. The pivot does not contradict the November 2025 position; Torvalds' framework was always "AI is a productivity tool, disastrous in production without oversight, useful for smaller or experimental work." Using AI on a personal hobby project falls squarely within the "fine" category he articulated. But the update is worth naming, because the rhetorical framing of "even Torvalds is sceptical" is now incomplete — the more accurate framing is that Torvalds distinguishes use cases carefully, and the distinction maps onto the mechanical architecture.

## 8. Why Programmers and Artists Responded Differently

A widely-observed cultural asymmetry: the software industry has largely embraced AI coding tools, while the art community has largely resisted AI image generation. The same technology, built on the same mathematical mechanism, trained on the same kind of data — other people's work, scraped, synthesised, and offered back as a service — has provoked opposite professional reactions. The mechanical explanation is part of the story, but not all of it.

The mechanical part is real. In programming, the output of an LLM is testable. Code either compiles or it does not. It either passes the test suite or it does not. The programmer can verify the prediction, reject it if it is wrong, and use it as a productivity accelerator when it is right. The next-word predictor is being used as a *tool* under human supervision. In image generation, the output is the final product. There is no compiler, no test suite, no objective verification step. When a diffusion model generates an image, the prediction *is* the work. The relationship between the prediction and the professional is structurally different.

But the mechanical account is incomplete. Other factors matter, and honesty demands naming them. Programmers generally own their own tools and produce outputs for employers on contractual terms; artists in the online commission economy depend on direct consumer relationships and on stylistic differentiation that AI most directly threatens. Programmers have long operated in a culture of remix, reuse, and open-source sharing; the art world has stronger norms around individual authorship and stylistic ownership. Artists were, in many cases, able to identify specific AI tools trained on identifiable artists' portfolios; programmers typically train on aggregated code where authorship attribution was already diffuse. The mechanical testability argument explains part of the asymmetry. The economic and cultural asymmetries explain the rest. Any account that relies purely on one of these dimensions is oversimplified.

## 9. The Bubble, the Capex, and the Consumers Who Pay Without Using

Torvalds' broader observation about the industry bears repeating directly. He described himself as a believer in AI as a technology but not in the market and marketing surrounding it. He predicted there will be a crash, and it will be ugly (Torvalds, 2025). This is not a contrarian take. It is consistent with the view of economists and industry analysts working from the actual financial data, which is worth laying out explicitly because it changes what the bubble argument actually licenses.

The scale is unprecedented. The five largest US hyperscalers — Amazon, Alphabet, Microsoft, Meta, and Oracle — are collectively committing approximately **$660–690 billion in capital expenditure for 2026**, nearly doubling the $387–405 billion spent in 2025. Amazon leads at $200 billion, Alphabet at $175–185 billion, Microsoft at $120 billion+, Meta at $115–135 billion, Oracle at $50 billion (Futurum, 2026; DatacenterDynamics, 2026). J.P. Morgan estimates total AI and data centre infrastructure investment will exceed $5 trillion over the next five years. Achieving a 10% return on that investment would require approximately $650 billion in new annual revenue — equivalent to every iPhone user paying an additional $34.72 monthly, or every Netflix subscriber contributing $180 per month, in perpetuity (J.P. Morgan, 2025, via Techstrong).

The ratio of spend to revenue is where the bubble case sharpens. In 2025, OpenAI reached approximately $13 billion in annualised revenue. Anthropic reached $9 billion by year-end 2025 and $30 billion by April 2026. Both are growing rapidly. But combined, that is a fraction of the hundreds of billions being invested annually in infrastructure. UBS data indicates that hyperscaler capex in 2026 will consume **nearly 100% of operating cash flows**, compared to a ten-year average of 40% (Introl, 2026). Pivotal Research projects Alphabet's free cash flow will fall roughly 90% in 2026 to $8.2 billion from $73.3 billion in 2025. Amazon is projected to run negative free cash flow of $17–28 billion. Meta's share-repurchase capacity is similarly constrained (CNBC, 2026; DatacenterDynamics, 2026). The companies are not going broke — balance sheets remain strong — but the game-theoretic dynamic is unmistakable: each is spending essentially all of its operating cash on AI infrastructure to avoid being left behind, and none has a clear path to revenue that justifies the spend in the near term.

The historical comparison is telling. Morningstar and Sparkline Capital's analysis finds that current AI capex already exceeds the relative-to-GDP peak of the dot-com boom, and when adjusted for the shorter useful life of AI chips (approximately one year of competitive relevance versus decades for physical infrastructure), it surpasses even the railroad buildout of the 1860s–1870s. Past infrastructure booms have reliably produced overinvestment, excess capacity, and poor returns for the infrastructure builders — even when the underlying technology delivered on its transformative promise. Telecom stocks crashed 92% after the dot-com bust and remain 60% below their peak twenty-five years later, despite the internet being exactly the transformation investors expected (Morningstar, 2025).

## 10. The Consumer Externality: How Non-Users Pay for the Buildout

Here is a dimension of the bubble that deserves careful naming, because it complicates the usual framing of "AI users subsidise AI development." A large portion of the real economic cost of the AI infrastructure buildout is being paid by consumers who have never used AI — and this is the strongest version of the "AI affects people who don't use it" argument, considerably stronger than most iterations it has taken in culture-war discourse.

The mechanism is memory fabrication capacity. High Bandwidth Memory (HBM), used in AI accelerators like NVIDIA's Blackwell B200, consumes approximately three times the wafer capacity per gigabyte compared to standard DRAM. Samsung, SK Hynix, and Micron — the three dominant memory manufacturers — have reallocated capacity toward HBM because its margins are substantially higher than consumer DDR5 (Network World, 2026; IDC, 2026). Micron exited the consumer memory market entirely in late 2025, concentrating on enterprise and AI customers. SK Hynix reported its HBM, DRAM, and NAND capacity "essentially sold out" for 2026.

The consumer impact is not speculative. According to Tom's Hardware's price tracking and Counterpoint Research analysis, a 32GB DDR5 memory kit that sold for around $87 in June 2025 was selling for $379 by January 2026 — a more than 4x increase within six months. 32GB DDR4 kits went from $60–90 to $150–180 in the same period. Contract DRAM prices rose 50% in the first three quarters of 2025, another 30% in Q4, and a further 20% expected in early 2026. DRAM prices rose approximately 172% throughout 2025. Samsung raised 32GB DDR5 module contract pricing from $149 to $239 — a 60% hike in a single adjustment. The industry has taken to calling the phenomenon "RAMageddon," and Micron's CEO has confirmed HBM and commodity memory shortages will extend into 2027 at minimum (Tom's Hardware, 2026; Network World, 2026; IDC, 2026).

This is a measurable, non-theoretical cost borne by everyone buying a PC, laptop, smartphone, or any device with significant memory — whether or not they have ever issued a single AI query. HP disclosed in its Q1 2026 earnings call that memory costs now account for 35% of PC build materials, up from 15–18% the previous quarter. IDC projects the PC market will contract 10–11% in 2026 and smartphones 8–9%, specifically because of the memory reallocation. Framework has issued public warnings about continued price pressure, and Lenovo's CFO described the cost surge as "unprecedented." Memory manufacturers are reallocating capacity away from consumer electronics toward AI infrastructure, and the consumer market is paying the difference.

> This is the legitimate form of the "AI affects non-users" argument, and it is one of the few versions of it that actually survives scrutiny. The consumer paying an extra $200 for their PC RAM is paying a real cost attributable to the AI infrastructure buildout, without having personally used AI or made any choice that meaningfully influenced the decision. But notice what this argument licenses: it licenses regulation of hyperscaler capital allocation, antitrust scrutiny of memory-market concentration, industrial policy to expand fab capacity, and consumer-protection considerations in how the capex race is financed. It does not license shaming individual AI users. The user who generates a birthday card is not the one deciding to reallocate Micron's wafer capacity to HBM. That decision is made in boardrooms by people pursuing 40%+ HBM margins. Misdirecting moral energy at individual users actively weakens the case for the structural reforms that would address the externality.

## 11. What Survives the Correction

The technology will likely survive a bubble correction. The transformer architecture is real, the mathematics works, and the useful applications — code prediction, translation, summarisation, search, accessibility — do not depend on the most speculative claims about near-term AGI. Anthropic and OpenAI are both generating real revenue at growth rates essentially without precedent in enterprise software history. Anthropic's jump from $1 billion ARR in January 2025 to $30 billion by April 2026 is, by analyst reports, faster than any tracked software company has ever reached that scale (SaaStr, 2026; Sacra, 2026). This is not vapour. Real businesses are paying real money for real products that solve real problems.

What will not survive a correction is the valuation structure, the more speculative product promises, and the companies whose business models assume continued exponential capability growth without commensurate cost reduction. The "AI wrappers" — companies providing thin layers over foundational models with no moat and no defensible pricing power — will be the first casualties, as Reddit's former CEO Yishan Wong predicted in late 2025 and as Semafor documented in early 2026. The consulting firms rebranded as AI companies will follow. The startups that raised on vision without distribution will follow them. The hyperscalers will absorb the pain via depreciation charges, debt-service costs, and constrained buyback programs, but their core businesses are not at existential risk.

The durable externalities are worth naming because they tell you what the buildout will leave behind even in the worst case. The SMR nuclear financing commitments (covered in the Environmental Explanation) will produce firm carbon-free baseload power through the 2030s regardless of what happens to AI revenue. The fibre networks, data centres, and cooling infrastructure being built will be available for whatever compute workloads come next — the same way dot-com-era fibre made YouTube possible in 2005. The trained research and engineering talent will not evaporate; it will move to the next wave of applications. And the transformer architecture itself is now part of the permanent computational vocabulary.

Distinguishing the durable technology from the speculative industry is what engineering literacy looks like in practice. Torvalds' framework — believer in the technology, sceptic of the market — is not a contradiction. It is the only position the actual data supports. And the implication for the culture war is the same as it has been throughout this essay series: the bubble is real, the overinvestment is real, the consumer externalities are real, and *none of these facts produce a case for shaming individual AI users*. They produce a case for regulation of institutional capital allocation, which is where responsibility for the outcome actually sits.

## 12. The Verdict: A Machine That Predicts, With Edge Cases

The anti-AI camp sometimes wants to say: "It's just autocomplete. It's stupid. It can't do anything." This understates the capability — the mathematics of attention, superposition, and high-dimensional embedding spaces produces results that are genuinely useful, often strikingly so. The pro-AI camp sometimes wants to say: "It understands. It creates. It reasons." This overstates the mechanism — it predicts the next word, stores facts as matrix products, and generates "creativity" by sampling from a temperature-scaled probability distribution.

By understanding the mechanism, we arrive at the honest position: **these models are powerful tools that are fundamentally shaped — and limited — by what they are.** They excel at local prediction. They fail at global coherence. They compress knowledge through superposition in ways that are qualitatively different from either human memory or database storage — but not so different that training-data memorisation is impossible, as Carlini et al. (2023) demonstrated. And they produce outputs whose apparent creativity is a mathematical artefact of probability-weighted sampling.

Three concurrent realities deserve acknowledgement.

**Mechanically,** a large language model is a transformer — a stack of attention blocks and MLPs, containing hundreds of billions of learned parameters, that converts input text into a probability distribution over possible next words. The parameters encode compressed statistical patterns from the training data, stored via superposition in high-dimensional vector spaces — with demonstrable but bounded memorisation of specific training examples. The entire process is matrix multiplication, non-linear activation functions, and softmax normalisation. It is mathematics, not magic, and not a clean separation from source material.

**Practically,** the tool is genuinely useful for professionals who understand its limits. Torvalds' framework — fine for exploration and on-ramps, disastrous in production without human oversight at system scale — maps to the architecture. The practitioners who use it well treat it as a local-prediction accelerator, not a global-coherence substitute.

**Culturally,** the market surrounding AI is showing bubble signatures, and a correction is likely. The technology will survive the correction because the underlying capability is real. But the hype, the valuations, and the breathless promises of imminent general intelligence — all of that is marketing built on top of a next-word predictor that has specific, architectural strengths and specific, architectural limits. Understanding the mechanism is the vaccine against both irrational fear and irrational hype.
